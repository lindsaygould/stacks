## 6. Fedir Kuznetsov

One clinical observation from our EEG-informed workflow

After several posts about the engineering logic of our project, I would like to show how this approach looked in one case selected from a series of practical clinical observations.  
This was not the only practical observation. I selected this particular case because its EEG spectral features were especially clear and useful for illustrating the workflow of the method.  
The workflow included:  
EEG recording;  
mathematical processing;  
comparison of selected electrode pairs;  
spectral analysis;  
calculation of an individual stimulation signal;  
stimulation course;  
follow-up assessment.  
The case concerned a 42-year-old woman with pronounced symptoms of depression. Before the method was applied, she had consulted a psychiatrist and received medication. Her baseline Beck Depression Inventory score was 44.  
EEG was recorded using a 16-channel TREDEX Expert system with the standard 10/20 electrode-placement scheme. When spectra and spectral power were compared, significant differences were identified in the F7–F8 and P3–P4 electrode pairs.  
Using Matlab scripts, a spectrum-difference file was generated based on the discrepancies identified between the selected electrode pairs. Differences in periodograms were also analyzed to clarify the signal structure and provide additional information for calculating the stimulation signal.  
  
Based on this analysis, an individual 30-minute stimulation file was created, and a stimulation course was carried out. The course included 10 sessions. After the eighth session, the Beck Depression Inventory score decreased from 44 to 9.  
This result is interesting because it demonstrates the full practical chain of the approach: from EEG data to signal calculation and follow-up assessment.  
At the same time, it should be interpreted with caution. This was a clinical observation, not a controlled clinical trial.  
The influence of concomitant treatment, the natural course of the condition, psychotherapeutic factors, patient expectations, professional attention, and other variables cannot be excluded.  
There was no control group, no sham stimulation, and no unified statistical protocol for the whole patient group.  
For this reason, the next step should be a properly designed pilot study with predefined inclusion criteria, validated rating scales, repeated EEG, adverse-event monitoring, follow-up, and — if feasible — control or sham stimulation.  
Important clarification: this is not a finished medical device and not a proven treatment method. It is a research hypothesis and an engineering-clinical prototype that requires independent validation.

## 9. Soroush Sarabi
**Links:** https://soroushsarabi.com/blog/posts/2026-06-23-what-eeg-foundation-models-tell-us-about-your-own-data · https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20812 · https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20673 · https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.02636

The secret to building better foundation models is not massive compute, it is a smarter inductive bias.

  
Standard foundation model recipes are predictable: tokenize the input, mask a portion, and train a transformer to guess the rest. But when you apply this brute-force approach to continuous, highly asymmetrical signals like brain activity, the architecture actively fights the data. Three new papers on EEG foundation models reveal what happens when you design for the signal instead of defaulting to scale.  
  
Rather than chopping continuous waveforms into arbitrary patches, models like BFM2 and REST-GAN process raw signals directly. By using techniques like continuous-time flow matching and generative adversarial networks, they respect the temporal dynamics of the brain. BFM2 even factorizes its architecture to preserve the spatial topology of scalp electrodes while heavily downsampling the time axis. The payoff is massive: it achieves state-of-the-art results using thirty times less pretraining data than competing models.  
  
But there is an uncomfortable systems implication. These efficient architectures are now generating synthetic brain signals so realistic that board-certified neurologists cannot distinguish them from real patient data. Simultaneously, models like NeuroShield are attempting to turn EEG into a device-agnostic biometric for identity authentication. The liveness and spoofing arms race that plagues facial recognition is arriving early for neural interfaces.  
  
The overarching lesson for applied machine learning teams is clear. If your signal is continuous, asymmetric, or scarce, aligning the model to the data will consistently outperform throwing more hardware at a generic transformer. You do not always need a bigger model to ship a reliable product; you just need a better-shaped one.

I'm building EEG/ERP analysis software that reads brain activity as the interference among many distributed networks, rather than as single regions or isolated components. The Quantified Soul suggests this is closer to how the brain (and the psyche it filters) is actually organized. 

    
The top panel is a typical ERP response—what gets recorded when a brain responds to a stimulus.  
  
But that wave is a sum. It's the combined output of many networks firing at once, constructively and destructively interfering with each other to produce what shows up at the surface.  
  
The bottom panel is the new view: which of those underlying networks are contributing at each moment. The wave above is the interference; the bars below are the networks doing the interfering.  
  
Karl Pribram was reaching for this fifty years ago—the brain as an interference pattern, not a circuit diagram. The conceptual frame has caught up to him; the clinical tools mostly haven't.  
  
There are real compromises to doing this with limited data, but directionally, this is what I'm building toward. We're constituted by an interference of organized processes, not by individual generators—and the instrument should be built to read that.

1\. Low-frequency right DLPFC stimulation acutely reduced functional connectivity at a broad, whole-brain level.  
2\. This disruption included within- and between-network connectivity across the salience, control, and default mode networks.  
3\. Acute functional connectivity modulation during a single TMS-fMRI session was associated with clinical improvement after the full course of treatment.  
4\. Predictive models based on salience-network-related modulation performed comparably to whole-brain models in predicting treatment outcomes.  
  
Taken together, these findings suggest that TMS may exert antidepressant effects not only through focal modulation of the stimulation target, but through acute perturbation of distributed brain networks. The salience network may be particularly relevant for understanding — and potentially predicting — clinical response.  
This work also highlights the value of concurrent TMS-fMRI as a causal neuroimaging approach: it allows us to move beyond correlational biomarkers and directly examine how brain stimulation changes network dynamics in vivo.  
As always, there are important limitations, including the modest sample size, atlas-based network definitions, and specificity to low-frequency right DLPFC protocols. An electroencephalogram is not a “photograph of thought”.  
It does not automatically diagnose a patient, and it does not directly tell us how to treat a specific condition.

In our project, we use EEG not as a standalone diagnostic tool, but as a source of measurable signal data:  
\- frequency structure;  
\- spectral power;  
\- differences between electrode positions;  
\- asymmetries between functionally related areas;  
\- changes before and after stimulation.  
This is important because transcranial stimulation is intended to influence neural activity. From an engineering point of view, it seems reasonable to first measure the activity pattern of the specific person before choosing where and how to stimulate.

1\) Record EEG.  
2\) Process the signal.  
3\) Compare selected regions.  
4\) Identify spectral differences.  
5\) Estimate possible target areas.  
6\) Calculate stimulation-signal parameters.  
7\) Verify changes after the course.  
The key point is to add a measurement and calculation stage before stimulation.  
This approach may help move from a “standard protocol for everyone” toward a more individualized model of transcranial stimulation.  
Important clarification: The approach requires independent technical testing, formal documentation, regulatory assessment, and controlled clinical validation.

## 36. Ildar Rakhmatulin
**Links:** _(none in the post — Ctrl-F the text below)_

More data and deeper algorithms don't automatically mean better EEG.

More channels. More data. More parameters.  
But quantity isn't quality — and the more I work in this field, the more obvious that becomes for me.  
Signal quality is the foundation. Everything else is downstream of it.  
Stable electrodes, reliable hardware, clean acquisition — these aren't boring engineering details. They're the difference between useful data and expensive noise.  
We now have impressive foundation models like ZUNA and REVE (trained on 92 datasets across 25,000 subjects). I just finished the test for the[ PiEEG](https://www.linkedin.com/company/pieeg/) dataset.   
The benchmarks look great. But here's the question:  
How do you actually verify that a model cleaned your signal — rather than modeled over it?  
Removing artifacts is not the same as preserving neural information. Noise floor, spectral power distribution, SNR — these are indirect metrics. A model can be optimized to look good on all of them while quietly replacing real signal with learned priors.  
The most honest validation would be evoked potentials: if the model preserves ERP timing and amplitude on held-out paradigms it was never trained on, that's signal. But even then, you need to be certain the evoked response wasn't reconstructed from context — not recovered from the electrode.  
  
Foundation models trained on lab-grade data are genuinely impressive. But most real-world EEG projects don't look like the lab. Different hardware. Different populations. Different noise profiles etc and more.  
Until we close that gap — with better hardware, honest benchmarks, and validation that goes beyond artifact metrics — the field will keep celebrating benchmark performance while struggling with deployment.  
Now I really believe (in spite of being a big fan of AI) in EEG Signal quality first, models second (Yes, you can make an ideal model for the control group, but make it universal for everyone, even with large data).

LLM Knowledge Bases

  

Something I'm finding very useful recently: using LLMs to build personal knowledge bases for various topics of research interest. In this way, a large fraction of my recent token throughput is going less into manipulating code, and more into manipulating knowledge (stored as markdown and images). The latest LLMs are quite good at it. So:

  

Data ingest:

I index source documents (articles, papers, repos, datasets, images, etc.) into a raw/ directory, then I use an LLM to incrementally "compile" a wiki, which is just a collection of .md files in a directory structure. The wiki includes summaries of all the data in raw/, backlinks, and then it categorizes data into concepts, writes articles for them, and links them all. To convert web articles into .md files I like to use the Obsidian Web Clipper extension, and then I also use a hotkey to download all the related images to local so that my LLM can easily reference them.

  

IDE:

I use Obsidian as the IDE "frontend" where I can view the raw data, the the compiled wiki, and the derived visualizations. Important to note that the LLM writes and maintains all of the data of the wiki, I rarely touch it directly. I've played with a few Obsidian plugins to render and view data in other ways (e.g. Marp for slides).

  

Q\&A:

Where things get interesting is that once your wiki is big enough (e.g. mine on some recent research is \~100 articles and \~400K words), you can ask your LLM agent all kinds of complex questions against the wiki, and it will go off, research the answers, etc. I thought I had to reach for fancy RAG, but the LLM has been pretty good about auto-maintaining index files and brief summaries of all the documents and it reads all the important related data fairly easily at this \~small scale.

  

Output:

Instead of getting answers in text/terminal, I like to have it render markdown files for me, or slide shows (Marp format), or matplotlib images, all of which I then view again in Obsidian. You can imagine many other visual output formats depending on the query. Often, I end up "filing" the outputs back into the wiki to enhance it for further queries. So my own explorations and queries always "add up" in the knowledge base.

  

Linting:

I've run some LLM "health checks" over the wiki to e.g. find inconsistent data, impute missing data (with web searchers), find interesting connections for new article candidates, etc., to incrementally clean up the wiki and enhance its overall data integrity. The LLMs are quite good at suggesting further questions to ask and look into.

  

Extra tools:

I find myself developing additional tools to process the data, e.g. I vibe coded a small and naive search engine over the wiki, which I both use directly (in a web ui), but more often I want to hand it off to an LLM via CLI as a tool for larger queries. 

  

Further explorations:

As the repo grows, the natural desire is to also think about synthetic data generation + finetuning to have your LLM "know" the data in its weights instead of just context windows.

  

TLDR: raw data from a given number of sources is collected, then compiled by an LLM into a .md wiki, then operated on by various CLIs by the LLM to do Q\&A and to incrementally enhance the wiki, and all of it viewable in Obsidian. 2\) To remember what I read  
3\) To be able to easily reference what I've read

## 76. Sohrab Rahimi
**Links:** https://arxiv.org/html/2603.20639v1

Most systems try to improve reasoning by pushing a single model harder. Larger models, longer chains of thought, more tuning. This is wrong.

  
In “Agentic AI and the Next Intelligence Explosion”, the Google Paradigms of Intelligence team points to something more concrete. Even within a single model, better performance shows up when the model implicitly runs multiple lines of reasoning, checks them against each other, and resolves disagreements. The model is not just extending a thought. It is coordinating several partial thoughts.   
  
 The paper goes further and reframes intelligence itself. What we call “intelligence” has never been a property of an individual unit. Human reasoning is already collective. Language, writing, institutions, and markets all externalize cognition across many participants. Large models are trained on that accumulated output. What sits inside the model is already a compressed form of social reasoning.  
  
This is why the internal behavior looks the way it does. When pushed to solve harder tasks, the model reconstructs interaction. It creates multiple viewpoints because that is the structure it learned from.  
  
Once you take that seriously, system design changes. The goal is no longer to build a stronger standalone agent. The goal is to construct a system where interaction is explicit and controlled. Agents are not just tools attached to a model. They become roles in a coordinated process. Each role operates with partial information and contributes to a shared outcome.  
  
The paper introduces a more dynamic view of this. Agents should be able to fork and recombine. A difficult task triggers expansion. The system creates multiple specialized instances, each working on part of the problem. When the work is done, results are merged and simplified. Complexity is handled by increasing interaction, not by increasing depth of a single reasoning path.  
  
There is also a shift in how alignment and control should be thought about. Current approaches assume one agent receiving feedback. That works when behavior is localized. It breaks when behavior emerges from many interacting components. The paper suggests moving toward institutional structures. Instead of aligning one model, you define roles, rules, and checks between agents, similar to how real organizations maintain control.  
  
Another point that often gets missed is that humans remain part of this system. These are not fully autonomous networks. They are mixed systems where humans and agents interact in different configurations. One human can coordinate many agents. One agent can serve many humans. Intelligence emerges from that joint system, not from either side alone.  
  
All of this leads to a different scaling law. You do not scale intelligence by increasing parameters alone. You scale it by increasing the number and quality of interactions between agents, and by structuring those interactions so that errors are surfaced and corrected.

His framing of AI as a co-pilot for physicians resonates with me. In the traditional model, medicine is often reactive and episodic, catching problems only after disease has already taken hold.  
  
AI can widen diagnostic thinking, reduce tunnel vision, and help ensure nothing critical is missed - especially when visits have to occur in 10-minute intervals to handle the sheer volume of patient needs. That is real progress.  
  
AI is not just a co-pilot for doctors. It can be a co-pilot for us and our loved ones.  
  
I know this personally. I lost my grandfather to a heart attack after he endured a quadruple bypass. I watched my dad go on a vicious health spiral and die a few years after his stroke. Those were not abstract statistics. They were warnings and lost time with my own family.  
  
Instead of waiting for my numbers to gradually worsen, I shared my family history of high cholesterol and cardiovascular disease with an LLM.  
  
The model walked me through the actual guidelines, the risk curves, and what sustained LDL exposure really means over decades. Together, we set a data-driven LDL target designed to materially reduce lifetime ASCVD risk. We reviewed the medicines that lower LDL, the particles that accumulate silently for years before suddenly stealing life from years.  
  
Then it built a structure around it.  
   
A tracker for every blood draw.  
Weekly reminders on workouts.  
Nutrition check ins.  
Trend lines over time.  
   
This is not episodic care, but continuous defense.  
  
Now imagine when lab results, wearables, nutrition data, and family history are seamlessly integrated. When risk models update dynamically as we age.  
  
Instead of seeing a doctor once or twice a year to react to what has already accumulated, we could have medical intelligence in our pocket, defending us in real time and minimizing the slow pile of risk that builds quietly until it becomes a heart attack, a stroke, or worse.  
  
AI absolutely enhances how medicine is practiced today, but its greatest promise may be upstream, shifting care from reactive to proactive, from episodic to continuous, and from disease management to true prevention.  
  
This is not about replacing doctors but about empowering patients, giving each of us the ability to understand our biology, act early, and change our trajectory before disease takes root.  
  
That is a profoundly optimistic future, and for the first time in history, it feels tangible, not theoretical.  
  
We are moving toward a world where medical intelligence is democratized, where fewer families must sit in hospital waiting rooms, replay the same preventable patterns, and lose loved ones unnecessarily early, the way I did.  
  
If we build it thoughtfully, with guardrails, with privacy, with physicians fully in the loop, it may become one of the most important public health shifts of our lifetime.

Creativity and flow aren't random. They follow patterns. And those patterns are different for every single person.

  
That's what brain data is starting to show us in ways we couldn't see before. The conditions that shape your best thinking aren't the same as the person sitting next to you. The time of day, the type of movement, the amount of caffeine, and the environment. All of it interacts with your brain in ways that are uniquely yours.  
  
For a long time we've tried to solve creativity and focus with universal prescriptions: Morning routines. Productivity frameworks. And while some of those things help some people, they miss the most important variable entirely.  
  
You.

And when you can see those patterns clearly, the decisions become a lot more informed. And a lot more human.

Prism is an Israeli-based company and they have a lot of data from an EEG and fMRI, some type of clinical study. They have all of this data mapping every single brain region so that they're able to predict what brain regions are firing via fMRI but from EEG alone. They basically did both at the same time so I've always correlated with what there's a machine learning model that I think the founders of Prism applied to it. Now EEG is able to predict amygdala and limbic regions specifically for PTSD but they're expanding into depression with Anonia, or the lack of ability to feel joy, and they're going from there.

  

What I did before my demo was:

1\. First there were some baselines, like "Look at this asterisk for a minute. Close your eyes and open them once you hear a ding."

3\. Then I was shown the exact same scene but I was supposed to be applying a mental strategy.

4\. In real time there was a three-second difference between my brain activity. If I was able to downregulate my amygdala, the people would start sitting down, the room would get quieter, and things like that.
