The lateral prefrontal cortex (LPFC) serves as a critical hub for higher-order cognitive and executive functions in the human brain, coordinating brain networks whose disruption has been implicated in many neurological and psychi- atric disorders. While transcranial brain stimulation treatments often target the LPFC, our current understanding of connectivity profiles guiding these interventions based on electrophysiology remains limited. Here, we present a high-resolution probabilistic map of bidirectional effective connectivity between the LPFC and widespread cortical and subcortical regions. This map is derived from intracranial evoked potential analysis of 48 797 intracranial direct electrical stimulation runs across 759 implantations in 724 patients with refractory epilepsy (368 male, 354 female, two unspecified; mean age ± standard deviation: 24 ± 13.5 years). We mapped probabilistic con- nectivity between brain parcels with adaptive resolution—higher resolution in the LPFC in the hemisphere of interest and lower elsewhere—maintaining statistical power while achieving 95% average confidence interval of ∼0.03 for connectivity probability estimates. In addition, the significance threshold (P-value) for probabilistic connectivity was obtained from surrogate distributions. Overall, we observed remarkable symmetry between afferent and efferent connectivity patterns of the LPFC, with a slight preference for efferent connections (mean slope = 0.92 ± 0.09, mean R2 = 0.93 ± 0.025). For example, connections between the inferior frontal gyrus (IFG) and anterior cingulate showed notable directional asymmetry. The IFG strongly projected to most brain networks compared to other LPFC regions, with the strongest connectivity to the ventral attention network (0.26 ± 0.01 compared to values between 0.15 and 0.21 in other LPFC regions). Posterior dor- so-lateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) demonstrated stronger connectivity to brain networks compared to anterior DLPFC regions (e.g. 0.21 ± 0.01 versus 0.15 ± 0.01 for connectivity to ventral attention network), with the exception of the limbic cortex. All LPFC subregions strongly projected to the fronto-parietal (greater than 0.17) and ventral at- tention (greater than 0.15) networks, with moderate connections to the default network (between 0.1 and 0.15, with the maximum corresponding to superior DLPFC). Finally, latency analysis suggested that the left LPFC’s influ- ence on ipsilateral emotion-related regions is primarily polysynaptic, with particularly strong pathways from IFG to amygdala (0.16 ± 0.02) and hippocampus (0.12 ± 0.01). Taken together, these comprehensive connectivity maps provide a new detailed electrophysiological foundation for understanding the functional anatomy of LPFC and guiding targeted brain stimulation protocols.

Introduction

The lateral prefrontal cortex (LPFC) comprises the dorso-lateral pre- frontal cortex (DLPFC) and the inferior frontal gyrus (IFG), some- times referred to as ventral lateral prefrontal cortex,1 and encompasses Brodmann areas (BA) 44, 45, 46, 47, 9 and 10.1,2 It is a critical hub for higher-order cognitive processing, integrating multiple neural networks.3-7 Understanding the effective connect- ivity patterns of these brain regions is essential for apprehending their causal interactions and it may serve applied purposes such as developing targeted therapeutic interventions. Yet our current knowledge is limited by methodological constraints in measuring directed information flow between brain areas.

The DLPFC is a region in the lateral part of the superior and mid- dle gyri that includes BA9 and BA46.1 Functional MRI (fMRI) studies report that the DLPFC is an important hub of functional brain net- works responsible of multiple higher-order cognitive functions.3,4,6,8-12 The DLPFC is an important node of main func- tional networks such as the lateral fronto-parietal network,13 called the fronto-parietal network (FPN) in the Yeo et al.14 classification, which includes the LPFC, anterior inferior parietal lobule and mid- cingulate gyrus.15 The DLPFC is also part of the default network, the dorsal attention network (DAN) and the midcingulo-insular or sali- ence network, called the ventral attention network (VAN) in Yeo’s nomenclature.14 The DLPFC structural connectivity has been as- sessed by MRI tractography studies and post-mortem dissections.10,16-19 The DLPFC is part of the frontal longitudinal sys- tem, including the frontal superior longitudinal tract, which com- prises short U-fibres linking prefrontal areas (e.g. BA8) with motor and premotor regions (e.g. BA6),10 as well as longer fibres from the superior longitudinal fasciculus (SLF), particularly its anterior dorsal components and the SLF II, that connect frontal with parietal areas.16,17 Vertical interconnections within the prefrontal cortex are further supported by the frontal aslant tract (FAT), which links posterior DLPFC areas to medial frontal regions involved in action

initiation and cognitive control.10,16-18 The DLPFC also establishes direct and indirect connections with visual areas, the hippocampal system, and memory-related areas of the posterior cingulate cortex and the temporal lobe.16,19,20 The inferior fronto-occipital fascic- ulus (IFOF) connects the medial occipital lobe with the superior and rostral part of the DLPFC.17 The cingulum bundle links the DLPFC with areas primarily involved in decision-making and cogni- tive processing. Additionally, the arcuate fasciculus connects the middle and inferior temporal gyri with posterior regions of the mid- dle and inferior frontal gyri, including the inferior posterior por- tions of the DLPFC. Finally, the uncinate fasciculus establishes fronto-temporal connectivity between the rostral DLPFC and tem- poral pole, active in memory tasks integrating emotional mater- ial.16,17 Together, these major white matter pathways enable the DLPFC to integrate information across widespread brain regions, supporting its central role in high-order cognitive and emotional functions.

The IFG is another critical region of the LPFC with its own specia- lized roles in cognition and behaviour. It is anatomically located in the inferior part of the LPFC, comprising three subregions: pars op- ercularis (BA44), pars triangularis (BA45) and pars orbitalis (BA47).21 The IFG is bilaterally involved in a wide range of cognitive pro- cesses, such as language, inhibitory control, emotional regulation and executive functions.22 ,23 Structurally, the IFG is interconnected with several major white matter tracts. They include the FAT, which connects the IFG, particularly pars opercularis and pars tri- angularis, with the supplementary motor area and medial superior frontal cortex.10,18,21 The SLF connects the IFG with the parietal and temporal lobes, while the IFOF links it to the occipital cortex via the temporal stem.17 The arcuate fasciculus supports long-range con- nectivity between the posterior temporal cortex and the posterior IFG,10,21 and the uncinate fasciculus connects the anterior IFG to the temporal pole and limbic areas.10,16 These tracts establish com- plex networks, for example, with the frontal pole, superior and middle frontal gyri, anterior cingulate cortex, premotor cortex,

striatum, temporal regions and parietal regions.24 IFG’s connec- tions underlie its participation in the default network23 and FPN.25 The pars triangularis in particular has been identified as a connector hub interfacing the sensory/somatomotor network, the default network and the dorsal and ventral attention networks.26 Furthermore, posterior IFG shows connectivity with the amygdala, as part of the prefrontal-limbic network involved in emotion and social behaviour.27

Dysfunction of the LPFC has been linked to numerous disorders characterized by disruptions in cognitive control, emotional regula- tion and executive functioning. Treatments for these disorders in- clude brain stimulation aimed at reestablishing healthy network connectivity, with the DLPFC and IFG emerging as promising tar- gets. For example, transcranial magnetic stimulation is clinically approved in the USA and Europe for the treatment of major depres- sive disorder by targeting the DLPFC and is thought to normalize ab- errant neural connectivity.28,29

While fMRI and diffusion imaging studies provide insights into the functional and structural connectivity, they lack information on electrophysiological activity or direct measure of directionality in effective connectivity. These questions can be addressed by studying intracerebral evoked potentials (iEPs) elicited by direct electrical stimulation using either electrocorticography or stereo EEG (SEEG) in individuals with refractory epilepsy. However, these studies are typically performed on relatively limited cohorts, com- promising spatial precision at the group level. Enatsu et al.30 demon- strated two key findings regarding DLPFC connectivity: stimulation of the DLPFC produced responses in both medial prefrontal cortex and premotor regions, while DLPFC showed responses when the pre- supplementary motor area was stimulated. Regarding IFG connectiv- ity, they found that stimulation of its posterior portion (BA44, BA45) activated the inferior parietal cortex. Lacruz et al.31 found that stimu- lation of the frontal lobe produced widespread responses, with par- ticularly strong connections observed within the frontal lobe itself and to the ipsilateral orbital area. These stimulations also revealed projections from the frontal cortex to the ipsilateral temporal regions and the contralateral frontal cortex. While these early studies provided important initial insights into LPFC connectivity patterns, their limited scope highlighted the need for more comprehensive mapping using larger patient populations and higher spatial resolution.

Those are provided by our large-scale F-TRACT32-36 database, which contains SEEG responses to intracranial electrical stimulations, comprising data from over 1400 individuals with drug-resistant epi- lepsy. We published a whole-brain effective connectivity atlas32-35 de- rived from an earlier version of this database that has enabled other research groups to conduct studies on topics such as network strat- egies of brain signal propagation,37 novel computational methods of causal derivation of effective connectivity38 or the processing of emo- tional facial expressions.39 In this study, we leveraged this database, focusing on LPFC connectivity, by introducing an adaptive resolution approach, profiting from Lausanne2008 multiscale parcellations.40 This technique allows for well-delineated regions of interest, by de- scribing them with finer parcels, while coarser ones are used else- where. It thus balances anatomical specificity with robustness and ensures comprehensive spatial coverage. We applied this method to mark the LPFC and extend the atlas from conventional parcels to the notion of functional networks.14 We aim to provide the first com- prehensive, large-scale SEEG-based probabilistic map of LPFC effect- ive connectivity and to reveal distinct patterns of direct and indirect connections of both cortico-cortical and mesial-temporal structures, including the amygdala and hippocampus.

Materials and methods

SEEG data

The data analysed were from the F-TRACT project (https://f-tract. eu), which aggregated multicentre SEEG recordings from indivi- duals with drug-resistant epilepsy. The signals were recorded dur- ing low-frequency (1 Hz) direct electrical stimulation performed in the awake state, at rest32,33 (Fig. 1). All participants gave consent to undergo this clinical procedure and to share their data for research purposes under ethical guidelines of the International Review Board at INSERM (protocol number: INSERM IRB C14-18), in agree- ment with the Declaration of Helsinki. We used F-TRACT data sam- pling LPFC, which resulted in the selection of 759 exams performed in 724 patients (368 male, 354 female, two unspecified) in 21 centres. The mean age of patients was 24 ± 13.5 years (± standard deviation), and the mean number of contact points per implantation was 91 ± 47.5. We considered in total 69 031 implanted electrode contacts (Fig. 2A), 1 198 828 recordings from 48 797 stimulation runs per- formed in 77% cases with biphasic (the rest monophasic) pulses with mean width ∼1.1 ± 0.46 ms, current intensity 3.1 ± 1.2 mA and charge 3.2 ± 1.5 mC. SEEG contacts were located in the neocor- tex and adjacent white matter, as well as in the hippocampus and amygdala. Epilepsy type was mainly multilobar (33.1%), temporal (13% left, 10.9% right) or frontal (13.2% left, 12.6% right) (see the Supplementary material for a more extensive analysis). Stimulations were performed under a variety of medications (anti epileptics, and in a few cases, antidepressants, anxiolytics or anti- psychotics), as commonly used in clinical practice.41-43

LPFC definition and parcellation

To delineate regions of interest, we chose Lausanne2008 parcella- tion40 as it is multiscale, i.e. available in a number of resolutions, a higher number following ‘Lausanne2008’, indicating higher reso- lution. Using the Lausanne2008-125 parcellation scheme,44 the LPFC includes 10 parcels (seven DLPFC and three IFG) in the left hemisphere and 13 parcels (9 DLPFC and 4 IFG) in the right hemi- sphere. Our definition of the DLPFC corresponds to a slightly larger area than its classical definition1 (Supplementary material, ‘LPFC definition and parcellation’ section). The selection of parcel size is crucial for map generation due to the trade-off between spatial sampling and spatial resolution. As spatial resolution increases, spatial sampling—and consequently the signal-to-noise-ratio—de- creases. To illustrate this methodological challenge, we evaluated connectivity patterns across different Lausanne parcellation resolu- tions (Fig. 1D–F). We observed that while Lausanne2008-33 provides robust statistical power, with about 60% of the brain covered with data fulfilling our statistical requirements, for example, a minimum of 50 recordings per region, but with insufficient anatomical detail, Lausanne2008-500 offers high anatomical precision but suffers from sparse sampling and data loss, with less than 2% brain cover- age. Lausanne2008-125 stands in the middle, having good anatomical precision and coverage of 29.9% of the brain (Supplementary Fig. 6). The anatomical distribution of the stimulation and recording con- tacts in the brain is determined by the patients’ epilepsy type. As a result, some brain regions have lower sampling, making it overly de- manding to achieve both a fine-grained parcellation such as Lausanne2008-125 and robust statistical power.

To address this issue and balance specificity with robustness, while ensuring comprehensive spatial coverage, we introduced ‘adaptive resolution’, meaning we applied different parcellations to the LPFC and the rest of the brain. For the LPFC, we used high-

resolution Lausanne2008-125 and a combination of these parcels to subdivide the DLPFC into anterior-posterior and inferior-superior parts. The rest of the brain was parcelled in three ways. First, the entire left hemisphere, excluding LPFC, was merged into one re- gion. Second, using unmodified Lausanne2008-33. Third, Yeo et al.14 functional networks were constructed by merging Lausanne2008-250 parcels, excluding LPFC and surrounding re- gions to avoid proximity-based bias. The procedure of merging standard atlas parcels to construct custom segments is schematic- ally illustrated in Fig. 1G–I, which also provides the colour legend for the Lausanne2008-125 LPFC parcels used throughout the paper.

Second, to reduce volume conduction effects and conform to stand- ard neurological practice, we re-referenced signals to a bipolar montage. Third, the short (4 ms max) stimulation artefacts were re- moved with local blanking and interpolation, and band-pass filter- ing between 1 and 45 Hz was applied. While this low-pass filter may appear too stringent to detect the very early fast responses with maximum sensitivity, it is a conservative choice to rule out a var- iety of noises (e.g. line noise, motion artefacts and epileptic activity, including stimulation-induced high-frequency oscillations46). Then, responses to all pulses in a stimulation run were averaged, baseline corrected and z-scored with respect to the \[−400, −10\] ms interval before stimulation. Such a response was considered a sig- nificant iEP if its absolute value exceeded a z-score threshold of z = 5 within a chosen peak significance time window set to \[0, 100\] ms, unless stated otherwise. This choice was motivated by previous dedicated studies,33,36,47 and the results for other thresholds, which are discussed in the Supplementary material, ‘Signal processing and connectivity assessment’ section, are available for download. We considered only the first crossing of the z-score threshold after stimulation, unless such an event was registered simultaneously in at least 10% of bipolar recordings within a 4 ms time window. In such a case, to mitigate volume conductance further, we discarded all such simultaneous maxima, and the algorithm proceeded to subsequent peaks in the signal.

We refer to connections with a peak delay of \<50 ms as ‘fast,’ and those with peak delay within the \[100, 400\] ms time window as ‘slow’. Prior literature has shown that iEPs with short peak delays primarily reflect projections within the same network, whereas longer delays are more characteristic of inter-network connec- tions.48 Other authors have explained the fast peak delay responses (\<50 ms) as a consequence of monosynaptic postsynaptic49 connections49-51 and slower peak delay responses (generally con- sidered over 50 ms) to group mono- and polysynaptic connections through cortico-cortical and cortico-subcortical projections.50,52 To avoid overlap between these classes, we excluded the inter- mediate 50–100 ms range from our definitions. Supplementary Fig. 8 illustrates an analysis supporting this classification.

Next, parcels were delineated from the preoperative MRI, ac- cording to five Lausanne2008 parcellations differing in resolution. To relate anatomical parcels to electrode contacts, we co-registered the MRI with postoperative CT or MRI capturing electrode positions in the brain using the IntrAnat Electrodes software.53 To minimize incorrect assignments to hemispheres, we disregarded contacts closer than 2 mm to the midsagittal plane (1213 contacts removed). At the cohort level, all neural signals were grouped by their parcels of stimulated and recorded electrode contacts. Next, ‘probabilistic effective connectivity’ was computed between sets of two parcels as the number of significant iEPs divided by the total number of stimulation-recording pairs for the two given parcels. The signifi- cance of probabilistic connectivity was assessed using surrogate distributions, as proposed in our earlier work,33 where the basic principle is to generate multiple probabilistic connectivity values from fake stimulation events randomly distributed during the pres- timulation period of actual recordings. For a P-value = 0.001, the sig- nificance threshold for probabilistic connectivity was 0.03 for the \[0, 100\] ms time window, 0.03 for the \[0, 50\] ms window and 0.08 for the \[100, 400\] ms time window. Finally, the ‘estimated propagation la- tency’ between sets of parcels was computed for each parcel pair as the median time elapsed until the occurrence of the first peak above the z-score threshold (also referred to as ‘peak delay’). We re- port median peak delays, along with efferent connectivity maps for the left hemisphere, for stimulation of DLPFC and IFG unified

Statistical analysis

To ensure data quality, we introduced the following statistical checks: we required at least 50 recordings per connection35 and to minimize effects related to individual subject characteristics we re- quired that each connection had data originating from at least three different implantations. For each connection, we computed symmetric confidence intervals (CIs) according to the Agresti– Coull formula54 with significance level α = 0.05. Similarly, the re- ported iEP feature—peak delay—is stated only for cases where at least three implantations and at least 50 significant iEPs were con- sidered. The last condition means that for connections with an in- sufficient number of significant iEPs, the probabilistic connectivity can be reported, but not the delay. Connections and delays not ful- filling these conditions are shown in grey in the cortical maps and in white in the connectivity matrices. Finally, to compare afferent and efferent connections, we classified a pair of conjugate connec- tions as significantly asymmetric if they met two criteria: (i) they showed significance in the binomial proportions test (α = 0.05) with Benjamini-Hochberg false discovery rate control55; and (ii) their connectivity values differed by more than 0.1, ensuring effect size.

Results

Whole-brain afferent and efferent connectivity of the LPFC

First, we investigated ipsilateral projections into and out of the LPFC. We found the average whole-brain efferent and afferent con- nectivity for the entire left LPFC (merged as one region) to be 0.15 ± 0.0015 and 0.14 ± 0.0014, respectively. Moreover, for specific LPFC region connections with the rest of the brain as a whole, we show that the average efferent and afferent connectivity is generally greater for IFG (0.16–0.18 and 0.15–0.17, respectively) than for DLPFC (0.115–0.15 and 0.11–0.14; all CIs \<0.007), within which it is greater for the superior and posterior regions rather than for the in- ferior and anterior ones. A strong symmetry between the efferent and afferent connectivity patterns is also shown (Fig. 2B). While we observed substantial symmetry between hemispheres, differ- ences in the parcellation scheme between the left and right hemi- spheres precluded precise quantification of this symmetry.

We next examined the specific ipsilateral connectivity patterns of individual LPFC subregions, finding that both efferent and affer- ent connections were strongest for nearby areas (Fig. 2C). Stimulation of LPFC parcels consistently activated nearby regions, with particularly strong responses in the medial superior frontal re- gion. This activation was most pronounced following stimulation of ‘superior frontal six’ (0.34 ± 0.02) and ‘rostral middle frontal two’ (0.29 ± 0.02). We also identified robust connections to the med- ial parietal lobe (precuneus), again strongest from ‘superior frontal six’ (0.23 ± 0.05) and ‘caudal middle frontal’ regions (0.18 ± 0.03 and 0.12 ± 0.03 for areas three and one, respectively). These stimulation

sites also evoked responses throughout the cingulate cortex, with notably strong activation of ‘caudal anterior cingulate’, following stimulation of ‘pars opercularis one’ and ‘pars opercularis two’ (0.43 ± 0.04 and 0.43 ± 0.05, respectively). In contrast, we observed limited connectivity between LPFC and temporal regions, with the strongest connection from ‘caudal middle frontal two’ and ‘pars op- ercularis two’ to the banks of the superior temporal sulcus (0.2 ± 0.1 and 0.18 ± 0.045, respectively). The afferent connectivity patterns largely mirrored these efferent projections, though being generally slightly weaker, e.g. as in IFG-anterior cingulate connections. Right hemisphere LPFC stimulations revealed similar patterns of con- nectivity, with some stronger connections, e.g. from superior and caudal LPFC regions to the precuneus (Supplementary Fig. 1).

In summary, these findings reveal specificities of LPFC connect- ivity; posterior regions maintain strong local projections, superior frontal areas coordinate with medial structures, and the IFG exhi- bits robust connections to anterior cingulate regions.

Transmission delays and directedness of connections

Intracerebral evoked potential peak delays were used to estimate whether a significant iEP appeared due to a ‘fast’ (likely direct) or ‘slow’ (likely indirect) connection. Here, we considered a connec- tion to be ‘fast’ or ‘slow’ if the peak delay was below 50 ms or above 100 ms after stimulation, respectively. For the fast connections (Fig. 3B), as previously, we found a strong decay of connectivity with distance. When DLPFC was stimulated, we noted relatively strong fast connections towards ‘superior frontal’ (0.15 ± 0.005, me- dian peak delay 27 ms) and ‘caudal anterior cingulate’ (0.13 ± 0.01, 24 ms). Fast connectivity to the ‘inferior parietal’ (0.08 ± 0.016, 36 ms) slightly stood out from the distance-related decay trend. Median peak delays were distance-dependent, weakly anticorrelat- ing with probabilistic connectivity (Supplementary Fig. 4). For ex- ample, the connection from DLPFC to ‘caudal anterior cingulate’ was characterized by a short delay (24 ms). When the IFG was sti- mulated, we observed a strong response in the insula (0.2 ± 0.01) and, similar to DLPFC, a notable response in ‘caudal anterior cingu- late’ (0.17 ± 0.02) was observed. Interestingly, the response in the ‘precentral’ parcel was stronger than for DLPFC (0.15 ± 0.007 versus 0.09 ± 0.005) and characterized by a relatively short average peak delay (24 ms).

For slow connections (Fig. 3C), the dependence on distance was no longer observed. However, anticorrelation between the two measured quantities was still seen. For example, when DLPFC was stimulated, connections to ‘post central’ (0.08 ± 0.005) and ‘su- perior temporal’ (0.07 ± 0.006) were weak and characterized by rela- tively long delays (172 and 189 ms, respectively). Conversely, a strong connection to ‘caudal anterior cingulate’ (0.23 ± 0.01) had a relatively short median delay (144 ms). When the IFG was stimu- lated, this connection was also strong (0.23 ± 0.02) and had an even shorter median delay (136 ms). Overall, IFG stimulation yielded marginally stronger connections and shorter delays than DLPFC stimulation. Finally, LPFC connectivity to the amygdala and hippocampus appeared mainly to be slow and to come prefer- entially from the IFG. The slow connection strengths from the IFG to the amygdala and hippocampus were 0.16 ± 0.02 and 0.12 ± 0.01, re- spectively, whereas all fast connections to these structures were below 0.05. Connectivity from LPFC to brain functional networks

To understand how the LPFC interacts with established brain net- works, we analyzed its effective connectivity patterns towards brain areas constituting functional brain networks as defined by Yeo et al.14 (Fig. 4A). To do so, we segmented the LPFC into anter- ior/posterior and inferior/superior DLPFC and IFG to achieve appro- priate distribution of stimulation and recording sites (Fig. 4A). The LPFC showed strongest connections to VAN and FPN, with moder- ate projections to the default network (Fig. 4B). This pattern varied systematically across LPFC subregions, with VAN receiving particu- larly strong inputs from the IFG (0.26 ± 0.01), posterior DLPFC (0.21 ± 0.01) and superior DLPFC (0.2 ± 0.01), while inputs from anterior DLPFC and inferior DLPFC (both 0.15 ± 0.01) were notably weaker. Connections to the FPN showed less regional variation. Still, they maintained a similar pattern, with stronger inputs from posterior DLPFC, superior DLPFC and IFG (all approximately 0.22–0.23 ± 0.01) compared to anterior (0.19 ± 0.01) and inferior DLPFC (0.17 ± 0.01). Connections to the default network were more uniform across LPFC subregions (all between 0.10 and 0.15 ± 0.01). Posterior DLPFC exhibited stronger connectivity than anterior DLPFC across all functional networks, except for limbic regions. When examining a finer-grained parcellation of the LPFC (Fig. 4C), two regions emerged with distinct connectivity profiles. The ‘superior frontal six’ parcel showed consistently strong connections to all three net- works (VAN, FPN and default network), while the ‘rostral middle frontal two’ parcel exhibited stronger projections compared to ‘ros- tral middle frontal one’ across these networks. For the right LPFC (Supplementary Fig. 3), the analysis revealed VAN to be the most connected network, followed by the fronto-parietal and default networks. IFG is the most connected LPFC region to the VAN, soma- tomotor network and limbic network but shows lower connectivity to the DAN and visual network. Posterior DLPFC exhibits stronger connectivity than anterior DLPFC to the VAN, DAN, FPN and soma- tomotor network but weaker to the limbic and default networks. In summary, these findings suggest heterogeneous connectivity, with posterior and superior parts of the LPFC, along with the IFG, having preferential connections to attention and cognitive control net- works, while maintaining balanced communication with the de- fault network.

Discussion

Summary of findings

Our analysis of numerous intracranial evoked potentials allowed us to build extensive probabilistic effective connectivity maps of the LPFC. In contrast to diffusion and fMRI maps, these maps convey directly measured information about directionality of connections. Although online materials provide all connections, in this report we mainly focused the presentation of results on the efferent (out- going) connectivity of the left LPFC because (i) this side had slightly higher sampling than the right LPFC; and (ii) the efferent connectiv- ity is of most interest for the clinical domain, as DLPFC encloses the most used targets of stimulation for neuromodulation.56 We de- monstrated five key findings: (i) The LPFC exhibits both local and long-range connections across the brain, with efferent connectivity approximately 5% stronger than afferent connectivity, positioning

it as an effective target for network-wide stimulation, often deliv- ered to the left hemisphere.57-60 (ii) The IFG showed slightly stron- ger connectivity compared to the DLPFC. Within the DLPFC, posterior and superior regions demonstrated stronger projections to the rest of the brain. (iii) Analysis of peak delays of evoked poten- tials revealed that both connectivity strength and signal delay were influenced by the distance between stimulation and recording sites. (iv) Connections from the LPFC to limbic regions were pre- dominantly slow. They were stronger from the IFG than the DLPFC, involving robust connections to the ‘caudal anterior cingu- late’ cortex and slow projections to the rostral one. The DLPFC showed stronger fast connections to the inferior parietal lobe com- pared to the IFG. (v) Network analysis demonstrated the strongest LPFC connectivity with the ipsilateral VAN, FPN and default net- work, with the IFG and posterior/superior DLPFC showing particu- larly strong connections to VAN, while FPN connections arose from multiple LPFC regions, and default network connections re- mained relatively uniform. Together, these findings provide a de- tailed map of LPFC connectivity that can inform both basic research and clinical applications.

Limitations

Several methodological and practical limitations should be noted. The connectivity measures presented are estimates rather than ground truth, as the underlying mechanisms are not fully under- stood. Multiple factors likely contribute to the evoked potentials,32 but their relative contributions—such as the balance of excitatory and inhibitory processes—remain unclear. In particular, stimula- tion can affect both grey and white matter, with the latter potential- ly resulting in the activation of bypassing fibres and in antidromic action potential propagation.61 This may affect our estimates of connectivity patterns and of directedness. Regarding connectivity, we have already demonstrated that stimulation performed in white matter leads to higher connectivity probability estimates compared to grey matter stimulation, but with similar spatial patterns of con- nectivity.33 We confirmed here this finding and measured a correl- ation of 0.88 between connectivity matrices (results not shown). Regarding directness, the directness towards more LPFC efference reported at the beginning of the Results section was more pro- nounced for grey than for white matter stimulation (total connect- ivity weight of efferences greater than for afferences by ∼7% and ∼2%, respectively; results not shown). Both additional analyses suggest that the activation of bypassing fibres is highly probable, but with relatively moderate effects at the atlas level. Overall, SEEG-derived connectivity does not fully align with white matter architecture,35,52 leaving the mechanisms of signal propagation un- certain. Technical constraints also apply—our analysis focuses on the first peak and uses bipolar montage, though alternatives like white matter referencing were proposed.41 Also, the provided con- fidence intervals are constructed on the assumption of measure- ment independence.

Because the data come from individuals with epilepsy, disease-related factors may have influenced the findings, though this is likely mitigated by the large sample size and the varied path- ology locations across patients. To assess the validity of our find- ings in a healthy population, we repeated the analysis excluding the top 20% of each participant’s recordings with the highest rate of epileptic-like spikes, as identified by DELPHOS62,63 software. This threshold approximates the proportion of electrodes captur- ing pathological activity.64 The correlation of thereby obtained con- nectivity matrices with the ones shown in this work were higher

The spatial distribution of electrodes (Fig. 2A), determined by clinical requirements, creates sampling bias toward certain brain regions. Our results are inherently probabilistic, showing variance even at the individual level,65,66 and the amplitude of recorded po- tentials is strongly influenced by the distance between stimulation and recording sites.33,37,52,67 This effect may lead to false negatives, as illustrated by our inability to detect previously reported connec- tions between LPFC and the occipital lobe.19,64 Here, we observed some long-range connections to be stronger than certain short- range ones, e.g. the connection from caudal middle frontal DLPFC parcels to the inferior parietal lobe (Fig. 2C). As expected, the distance-dependent decay was not present for slow connections (Fig. 3C). While this decay may limit anatomical connectivity map- ping, it could be beneficial for guiding stimulation protocols, as it also appears relevant to stimulation effects.

Comparison to other studies and modalities

Our work aligns with earlier SEEG connectivity studies on subjects with epilepsy. For example, in line with Enatsu et al.30 we found that stimulation of the LPFC elicited a strong activation of the prefrontal cortex, and a weaker one in the dorsal and ventral premotor areas (Fig. 2C). Unlike them, we saw fast activation of the inferior parietal re- gion after stimulation of some subregions of the DLPFC. Similar to them, we found connectivity from the pre-supplementary motor cor- tex to DLPFC, mostly the caudal parcels. Our findings also align with those of Lacruz et al.,31 particularly regarding the connectivity from LPFC to ipsilateral orbitofrontal cortex and temporal lobe, the latter ones accounted for by rather slow than fast connections (Fig. 3).

Our analysis of connectivity also aligns with earlier research re- garding connectivity between LPFC and the amygdala, especially interesting due to its role in emotion regulation.68,69 In concordance with the findings of Ghashghaei et al.,69 we identified the anterior part of the IFG to be the most connected to the amygdala (Fig. 2C and E). We found these connections to be mainly directed from amygdala to IFG, which was not reported by Ghashghaei et al.69 in their study on rhesus monkeys with neural tracers. Furthermore, we found the IFG to amygdala connectivity to be mainly polysynap- tic (Fig. 3B and C), consistent with previous studies suggesting the ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC)27 as a relay on this path.

Structural connectivity analysis performed by Jung et al.12 in healthy participants suggested that the ventral-caudal regions of DLPFC (BA9/46va and BA9/46vp), unlike the dorsal-rostral ones (BA46 and BA9/46da67,68), connect with the parietal cortex. Our re- sults indicate the same (Fig. 2C and E). Note, however, that our def- inition of the DLPFC includes parcels posterior to their definition. On the other hand, the strongest connections we identified to the parietal cortex were to its inferior part, with only weak connections to the superior part, whereas Jung et al.12 reported the opposite. Their functional connectivity analysis suggested that a part of DLPFC is more connected to the default network and the other part to the FPN, whereas our results indicate that all of the LPFC is more connected to FPN than to the default network (Fig. 4B).

Network connectivity of the DLPFC was also studied by Cardenas et al.70 (patients with major depressive disorder) and Chen et al.71 (healthy participants) by means of fMRI connectivity. They suggested that the more anterior DLPFC exhibits stronger con- nectivity with VAN than FPN, whereas the more posterior DLPFC shows the opposite. Our results indicate that the anterior part of the DLPFC is more connected to FPN than VAN, and the posterior

part is connected fairly uniformly. Additionally, the posterior part is more connected to both FPN and VAN than the anterior part (Fig. 4B). It could be related to the higher temporal excitability34 of the posterior part.

Our work is also consistent with previous tractography studies in healthy participants. For example, we demonstrated weak fast connectivity from the DLPFC to the temporal lobe (Fig. 3B). Alves et al.19 found this connection to be polysynaptic. We also found moderate fast connections between the IFG and the temporal lobe, which is consistent with the anatomy of the uncinate fascic- ulus. Regarding slow connections to the temporal and parietal lobes (Fig. 3C), both the DLPFC and IFG showed strong connectivity, which could be associated with the polysynaptic pathway, using the un- cinate and inferior longitudinal fasciculus, and arcuate tracts and the superior longitudinal fasciculus II, respectively. The latter fas- ciculus could also account for connections between the DLPFC and the inferior parietal region (less prominent for the IFG). The medial connectivity pattern, i.e. stronger connectivity from LPFC to the cingulate and parietal cortices with weaker connectivity to the paracentral area, aligns with the cingulum bundle. Catani10 also explains the connections we found, showing connection of the posterior DLPFC to the inferior parietal through the superior longitudinal fasciculus I and II and the IFG to the supramarginal area through the superior longitudinal fasciculus III.

Relevance to clinical applications

Modern treatments of psychiatric disorders involve stimulation of the affected brain networks, usually achieved by targeting central hubs in the LPFC.23,27-29,72 Such is the case for major depressive dis- order, which has been associated with dysfunction of the FPN, DAN, VAN and default network.4,7,73 Indeed, our results confirm high connectivity from LPFC to FPN for all LPFC areas, with stronger connectivity to the inferior parietal lobe from caudal middle rostral (Lausanne2008-125) DLPFC parcels. Several studies have postulated that repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation should reach the vmPFC to be effective.27,74-76 In the Lausanne2008-33 parcellation used in our work, the vmPFC corresponds to the parcels ‘medial orbito frontal’, ‘rostral anterior cingulate’ and the anterior part of ‘su- perior frontal’. These parcels were most activated by stimulation of the ‘superior frontal six’ of the DLPFC and ‘pars triangularis one’ and ‘pars opercularis one’ of the IFG (Fig. 2C). To summarize, our results suggest that the dorso-caudal part of the DLPFC and the IFG could have slightly higher efferent connectivity than the rest of the LPFC, in particular to brain regions of interest in major depressive disorder, hence being possible targets in brain stimulation treatments.
