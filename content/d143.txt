1Circuit-level computational models can do more than explain existing data; they can generate novel hypotheses and capture individual 2differences in human populations. We demonstrate this using a biophysical corticostriatal model, transforming simulated neuronal activity 3from local field potential (LFP) into functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) signals via the balloon model, and finally generating 4behavioral outcomes. We then validate the results against those obtained from human subjects. The model generates a counterintuitive yet 5testable prediction. Although prefrontal–striatal coherence demonstrates an increase at the neural LFP scale during learning, the same circuit— 6passed through the hemodynamic transform—–predicts a decrease in prefrontal–striatal BOLD correlation, driven by category-selective 7representations that emerge in cortex but not striatum. This prediction is confirmed in fMRI data optimized for single-subject-level detection 8sensitivity. The model further enables single-subject fitting of a behavioral outcome, classifying individuals into those differentially responding 9to positive versus negative reward bias. Established biomarkers, including activation measured by the amplitude of low-frequency fluctuations 10and dopaminergic effects on hemodynamic latency, are also conserved across LFP and fMRI scales. In human neuroimaging, however, such models have been largely displaced by data-driven machine learning 8 approaches that achieve impressive performance in pattern recognition and feature extraction but are agnostic to the underlying 9 circuitry. These approaches cannot distinguish primary neural mechanisms from secondary or epiphenomenal effects, and 10 because they do not parameterize the generative process, they cannot produce labeled training data tied to mechanistically 11 defined phenotypes. 12 While strides have been made in bridging circuit-level neural models to functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) 13 blood oxygen level-related (BOLD) signals, none have closed the loop from independently validated neural-scale dynamics to 14 mechanistically interpretable cognitive effects in human subjects. Whole-brain spiking-network models do contain receptor- 15 specific synaptic detail (3–5), but their interface to fMRI is benchmarked against resting-state functional connectivity, a metric 16 that many different cellular configurations can match equally well, and they are not built around a systems-neuroscience-level 17 circuit with neurocognitive outputs. Mean-field and neural mass reductions (6–8) reach whole-brain scale only by collapsing 18 each region to one or two summary state variables. These models can be perturbed at an aggregate level to study circuit-level 19 dysfunction, as in work on schizophrenia-related thalamocortical impairment (9), but they cannot resolve how a specific 20 receptor subtype, cell class, or cortical layer would respond to an intervention. Task-specific circuit models couple biophysical 21 neural dynamics to a forward BOLD model by summing synaptic activity, or absolute synaptic currents, and convolving 22 with a hemodynamic response function (10–12). The neural dynamics range from spiking integrate-and-fire networks with 23 explicit AMPA, NMDA, and GABA currents (10) to population-level firing-rate units constrained by neurophysiology (11), 24 and the simulated BOLD reproduces qualitative features of task-evoked fMRI from the same paradigm. None of these models 25 is independently validated against electrophysiology from the circuit and task it represents, however, so matching simulation 26 outputs to those derived from fMRI BOLD does not by itself establish that the underlying neural dynamics are correct. Underlying all of these approaches, the hemodynamic interface itself remains 30 unresolved at the cellular scale: the Balloon-Windkessel family does not specify which aspect of neural activity drives BOLD 31 (17), and richer formulations incorporating astrocytic calcium and neuro-glio-vascular pathways (18) have thus far been shown 32 only in single-area implementations. The result is a literature in which circuit models reproduce coarse fMRI features but 33 have not yet been shown to deliver on what makes such models worth building in the first place: mechanistic predictions that 34 survive translation to human data and can be used to interpret individual subjects. 35 The stakes of closing this gap go beyond methodology. A circuit model that survives translation to human fMRI provides 36 something that no purely statistical approach can: a generative account in which individual differences are expressed as 37 differences in mechanistically grounded circuit parameters rather than as associations. That distinction matters for applications 38 like predicting how a novel drug acting on a specific receptor target would alter the BOLD signal, or interpreting the cellular-scale 39 consequences of a lesion or a developmental difference. Without such a model, fMRI in humans is restricted to description. 40 With one, the same imaging data becomes a powerful tool for simulating counterfactuals. 41 With this challenge in mind, we translate a corticostriatal circuit model previously independently validated (2) against 42 macaque electrophysiology (19) into simulated BOLD using only two transformations: the Balloon-Windkessel hemodynamic 43 model converts the simulated neural activity into a synthetic BOLD signal, and bandpass filtering constrains it to the temporal 44 frequencies of a real fMRI acquisition. We then compare the resulting fMRI-scale simulations against empirical 7-Tesla fMRI 45 data from human participants engaged in category learning (Figure 1; N=31, N=62 sessions). In the Dot Category Learn Task, 46 category prototypes are defined by spatially distinct patterns of random dots. These prototypes, unknown to participants, are 47 inferred from ambiguous exemplars: dot patterns that are imperfect representations of each prototype. Using a forced-choice 48 design (Is this exemplar Category A or Category B?), participant choices are reinforced by positive and negative feedback 49 coupled to monetary reward (gaining money when they classify the exemplar correctly and losing money when they classify 50 the exemplar incorrectly). Three key mechanistic signatures of the circuit survive the translation across scales. For both the 51 simulation and human data, brain activity-based learning signatures localize to the prefrontal cortex, dopaminergic dynamics 52 are preserved, and learning-induced PFC-striatal functional connectivity decreases, a circuit-based consequence of increased 53 cortical category selectivity. This last result is particularly striking because it demonstrates how “functional connectivity,” as 54 understood at the local field potential and fMRI scales, can fundamentally differ in sometimes counterintuitive ways. More 55 generally, this surprising result suggests the value of a multi-scale model for generating mechanistically-derived biomarkers at 56 each scale “bottom-up” rather than starting from fMRI-derived statistical outcomes and relying on intuitions to infer their 57 underlying basis in terms of circuit function. 58 Moreover, the translation enables the estimation of a behavior-predictive biomarker that would have been unattainable 59

Because the circuit explicitly parameterizes distinct reward-learning 60 phenotypes, its simulation generates labeled ground-truth training data unavailable to either measured neuroimaging or 61 behavioral data alone. Here, we demonstrate that a behavioral classifier trained entirely on simulated data stratifies human 62 participants by reward bias, with stratification independently validated by dopamine trajectory differences that were withheld 63 from training. To our knowledge, this is the first demonstration that a circuit model validated at the neuronal scale can be 64 translated into human fMRI in a way that preserves its mechanistic content and yields individual-subject behavioral outputs. 65 This proof of concept addresses a structural limitation of data-driven approaches to human neuroimaging, illustrating how 66 biophysical circuit models can stratify human individual variation in circuit function. 67

Results 68

A. Conserving Dynamics: Dopamine. For the Dot Category Learn Task, the corticostriatal model’s dopamine dynamics have 69 previously been shown (2), to start at a high concentration and decrease as learned patterns are consolidated. Specifically, 70 the model predicts an elevated average dopamine level during early learning that converges to a baseline once participants 71 identify the prototypes, a trajectory conserved at the fMRI scale (Figure 2A). To compare to its equivalent measured during 72 fMRI, we used hemodynamic speed (HS; see Methods for details) as an empirically-established (20) surrogate for dopamine. HS 73 demonstrates the same decrease in dopamine from the learning to the learned state (standardized regression coefficient β = 0.57, 74 p = 0.002) (Figure 2C, D). This suggests that the dopamine-driven feedback diminishes as participants adapt to a task. While 75 the model’s dopamine effects on synaptic plasticity do not translate to fMRI, the dopamine dynamics themselves survive. This 76 suggests that dopamine could serve as a viable pathway for identifying and analyzing dysregulation in task adaptation. We also 77 observed a reset of this signal when the task rule changed, consistent with dopamine’s proposed role in signaling prediction error 78 upon environmental restructuring. Together, these results confirm that the model’s dopamine surrogate captures empirically 79 measurable dynamics, providing a validated foundation for examining how learning-related signals are expressed across the 80 broader corticostriatal circuit. Participants showed no significant difference in accuracy (z = −1.506, p = 0.1) from day 1 to day 81 2 (Figure S1). Hemodynamic speed (HS) demonstrated statistically significant but comparatively lower test-retest reliability 82 (r = 0.45, p = 0.01), consistent with its sensitivity to state-dependent factors such as reward perception (Figure S2A). 83

We then asked 88 whether these subgroups differed on independent fMRI-derived measures that were never considered during classifier design. 89 Of 31 participants, 18 were classified as negative reward bias and 13 as positive reward bias (Figure 3A). Having established 90 this partition purely from simulation, we then examined whether the groups differed in their empirical dopamine trajectories, a 91 signal orthogonal to the classifier’s training. In the simulation, negative reward bias is associated with a monotonic dopamine 92 decrease across learning, while positive reward bias is not (Figure 3B). Strikingly, this distinction was recovered in the empirical 93 data without ever being sought: negative reward bias participants showed robust declines in dopamine during learning (β = 94 0.66, p = 0.006), while positive reward bias participants exhibited variable trajectories (β = 0.50, p = 0.07; Figure 3C). Thus, 95 the simulation-trained behavioral classifier, applied to an independent empirical dataset, recovered subgroup differences in a 96 neurobiological signal that played no role in its construction. This suggests that reward bias reflects a genuine, mechanistically 97 grounded feature of dopaminergic learning dynamics, one identified by the model before it became visible in the data. Reward 98 bias classification did not affect HS test–retest reliability in either reward class (negative class: r = 0.46, p = 0.05; positive 99 class: r = 0.46, p = 0.1; Figure S2B). 100

C. Conserving Regions: ALFF. Beyond validating dopamine dynamics, we asked whether the model reproduces the regional 101 pattern of neural activity changes during learning. Two questions motivated this analysis: which regions show consistency 102 between the circuit model and empirical ALFF measurements, and given that the circuit was derived from macaque electrode 103 recordings, how do these signals localize in the human brain? To address this, we simulated ALFF changes between the learning 104 and learned states, finding a clear increase in anterior cortex and a smaller increase in striatum (Figure 4A). We mapped these 105 to the human PFC and caudate, respectively, based on their functional roles and dopaminergic signatures (21–23) (Figure 4C, 106 E, F). Empirically, ALFF increased significantly in superior frontal cortex (β= 0.53, p = 0.003) and middle frontal cortex 107 (β = 0.45, p = 0.01), with a smaller increase in medial frontal (β = 0.38, p = 0.04), localizing the circuit’s anterior cortex 108 signal primarily to medial and superior frontal regions (Figure 4B, D). Beyond these frontal regions, the voxelwise map also 109 showed learning-related ALFF increases in precuneus and posterior cingulate cortex (Figure 4B); these midline regions fall 110 outside the corticostriatal circuit modeled here and were not part of the localization target, but their engagement is consistent 111 with their established involvement in category learning and value-based representation. Caudate ALFF showed no significant 112 change (β = 0.27, p = 0.2), consistent with the model’s prediction of minimal striatal activity change during this transition. 113 This convergence between simulation and imaging suggests the null caudate result reflects a genuine mechanistic property of 114 the circuit rather than a sensitivity limitation of fMRI. ALFF showed strong test–retest reliability (r = 0.89, p = 2 × 10−11; 115 r = 0.79, p = 1 × 10−7; r = 0.89, p = 3 × 10−11 in frontal regions; and r = 0.71, p = 9 × 10−6; r = 0.77, p = 4 × 10−7; r = 0.77, 116 p = 5 × 10−7 in striatal regions), with the highest stability observed in regions exhibiting robust learning-related signatures 117 (Figure S3, S4). 118

D. Outcomes: Connectivity and Asynchrony in Brain Learning. The circuit model predicts that cortical and striatal dynamics 119 diverge as learning proceeds. Consistent with this, ALFF increases were confined to prefrontal regions. We next tested 120 whether this divergence extended to the functional connectivity between these structures. Comparing learning and learned 121 states revealed a consistent, statistically significant decrease in functional connectivity between prefrontal cortex and striatum 122 following task acquisition (Figure 5C). Across simulations, connectivity changes were heterogeneous, ranging from increases and 123 decreases to negligible shifts. Using the empirically observed decrease in connectivity as a filter on the stochastic simulation 124 space, we identified a subset of simulations whose dynamics matched the human data. These simulations were characterized 125 by significantly greater and more reliable category selectivity in PFC relative to striatum, compared to simulations showing 126 stable or increasing connectivity, motivating a formal analysis of preferential selectivity in the empirical data (Figure 5B). 127 Following learning, category selectivity increased in PFC, particularly in medial (β = 0.37, p = 0.04) and superior frontal 128 (β = 0.31, p = 0.08) regions, consistent with ALFF findings above and with model predictions, while striatal selectivity 129 remained statistically unchanged (Figure 5C). Functional connectivity between these PFC regions and striatum decreased 130 correspondingly (medial frontal–striatum: β = 0.43, p = 0.02; superior frontal–striatum: β = 0.35, p = 0.05). We propose 131 that this reflects a shift in PFC signal composition: prior to learning, PFC and striatum share a common input driving their 132 correlation; as learning proceeds, PFC acquires category-selective representations not present in striatum, reducing their shared 133 signal and decreasing functional connectivity even as PFC becomes more informative. These population-level findings raise the 134 question of whether individual variation in this corticostriatal divergence reflects meaningful differences in underlying circuit 135 parameters, a question we address directly by examining whether simulation-derived behavioral phenotypes predict distinct 136 neural signatures across participants (Figure 5A). As expected, preferential selectivity showed poor test–retest reliability 137 (r = −0.14, p = 0.4; r = 0.23, p = 0.2; r = −0.01, p = 1), reflecting variability in which stimulus category was preferentially 138 learned across sessions (Figure S5). Functional connectivity likewise showed limited test–retest reliability (r = 0.14, p = 0.4; 139 r = 0.33, p = 0.07; r = 0.30, p = 0.1), consistent with state-dependent learning differences (Figure S6). This dissociation from 140 the high between-session reliability of the trait-like ALFF and hemodynamic speed metrics makes sense: a metric that tracks 141 within-session learning-induced reorganization should not be strongly correlated across independent learning episodes from 142 different days, whereas regional indices of stable activity properties should be. We therefore interpret functional connectivity 143 and preferential selectivity as state markers of active learning, and ALFF and hemodynamic speed as more trait-like regional 144 indices. 145

The model predicts elevated dopamine during 152 early learning that converges to baseline once the task is acquired—a pattern confirmed in subjects, with dopamine levels 153 showing a consistent decrease from the learning to the learned state. It is important to note the 158 distinct roles played by different measures in this validation architecture. ALFF serves as the localization tool: because the 159 macaque-derived circuit nodes carry no direct anatomical label in human space, ALFF changes are used to identify which human 160 brain regions correspond to the simulated anterior cortex and striatal compartments. Once this localization is established, 161 functional connectivity and hemodynamic speed provide independent validation of the model in those regions—they are 162 methodologically distinct measures that were not used to define the regions of interest. In our model the inversion arises not from a change in the underlying coupling but 178 from the redistribution of variance under the hemodynamic transform (Eqs. 1–6): learning-related low-frequency power added 179 to PFC inflates its variance and lowers the BOLD correlation even as the same connectivity measure increases at the neural 180 (LFP) scale. Consistent with reflecting the neural dynamics rather than a particular forward model, substituting a canonical 181 hemodynamic response function for the Balloon–Windkessel transform did not substantially alter the result. Thus, our results 182 reinforce the power of multiscale modeling while exposing the limitations of intuitively assuming a one-to-one mapping between 183 synaptic plasticity and fMRI-derived connectivity. The spatiotemporal distinctions between scales matter not only because 184 mechanistic detection sensitivity is lost in fMRI, but more fundamentally because they determine which fMRI signal we should 185 look for. Moreover, one benefit of being able to pre-specify an fMRI biomarker mechanistically is that it also increases our 186 statistical power to detect it. 187 Finally, and most importantly, a behavioral classifier trained entirely on simulated data successfully stratified empirical 188 subjects into positive and negative reward bias groups. The negative reward bias group showed significantly steeper and more 189 consistent dopamine declines—a signal orthogonal to classifier training—providing independent biological validation of the 190 classification. In contrast, hemodynamic speed can be derived from standard 217 fMRI data, providing a scalable alternative that remains sensitive to learning-related dopaminergic dynamics predicted by 218 circuit models. 219 The inference linking hemodynamic timing to dopaminergic physiology is supported by both mechanistic and empirical 220 evidence. Dopamine modulates neurovascular coupling and vascular tone through D1- and D2-receptor–mediated effects, 221 influencing cerebral blood flow dynamics and thereby shaping the temporal characteristics of the BOLD signal. Consistent 222 with this mechanism, recent work has shown that voxel-wise hemodynamic latency recovers established striatal dopaminergic 223 gradients and correlates with independent measures of dopamine function, including PET-derived indices and pharmacological 224 manipulation (26). 225 Within this framework, the present caudate-specific findings provide convergent support for the validity of hemodynamic 226 speed as a surrogate for dopaminergic signaling. The progressive decrease in signal during learning and subsequent stabilization 227 in well-learned states are consistent with canonical dopamine dynamics associated with prediction error updating and value 228 stabilization. The sensitivity of the metric to these structured learning phases, particularly within the caudate, aligns with its 229 established role in integrating dopaminergic signals for adaptive learning within frontostriatal circuits. 230 More broadly, these findings suggest that dopamine could serve as a viable pathway for identifying dysregulation in task 231 adaptation, even in contexts where the specific neural dynamics underlying that dysregulation are difficult to detect directly. 232 This is particularly relevant for conditions characterized by subtle or early-stage dopaminergic disruption, where conventional 233 biomarkers may lack sufficient sensitivity. Asymmetric 234 Specialization. To formalize this mechanism, we model the regional BOLD signals as composite time series with 235 separable components. Before learning: 236 PFC(t) = C(t) + εPFC(t) \[1\] 237 STR(t) = C(t) + εSTR(t) \[2\] 238 where C(t) represents common input signals (including arousal, global task engagement, dopaminergic modulation, and 239 correlated low-frequency hemodynamic fluctuations) and ε(t) represents region-specific, uncorrelated noise. In this initial state, 240 both regions are dominated by shared variance from C(t), resulting in high BOLD correlation (typically r ≈ 0.85 to 0.94 in our 241 data). When participants were behaviorally classified 271 into positive versus negative reward bias groups, distinct dopaminergic patterns emerged across learning states. In simulation, 272 where only phasic dopamine amplitude was manipulated, the negative reward bias group showed an overall higher dopamine 273 level compared to the positive group, along with a decreasing trend across learning. Because only phasic parameters were 274 altered, this baseline elevation likely reflects sustained reward prediction error signaling: slower value updating maintains 275 larger phasic bursts early in learning, which gradually diminish as contingencies stabilize (1, 35). In contrast, the positive bias 276 group exhibited a non-monotonic (up-down) dopamine trajectory, consistent with either efficient transfer of prediction error 277 signaling to cue-based value representations (1) or early salience amplification that normalizes with predictability (36, 37) - 278 two mechanistically distinct routes to the same behavioral outcome. 279 In human data, where the surrogate measure reflects caudate latency rather than amplitude (26), we do not observe a 280 robust overall level separation between groups. Instead, the defining feature is trajectory: the negative bias group shows a clean 281 monotonic increase in latency from early to learned states (p = 0.006), while the positive group shows a non-monotonic pattern 282 that does not reach significance (p = 0.07). Critically, this asymmetry is precisely what the simulation predicts: the model 283 generates a consistent, monotonic dopamine signature for negative reward bias and a variable, non-monotonic trajectory for 284 positive reward bias. The non-significance of the positive bias result is therefore not a failure of replication but the predicted 285 outcome—a group defined by trajectory variability should not, and does not, produce a consistent directional effect. This 286 predicted dissociation between groups, recovered in empirical data from a classifier that never had access to neural measures, 287 provides the key validation. We do not interpret this as evidence of the same underlying mechanism across species, but rather 288 as convergent support for the broader claim that reward bias indexes a genuine difference in dopaminergic learning dynamics 289 (38, 39). To bridge from this 388 mesoscale electrophysiological signal to the Blood-Oxygen-Level-Dependent (BOLD) signals measured by fMRI, we apply 389 the Balloon-Windkessel model (51, 62) to the simulated LFP output. Balloon model parameters follow the experimentally 390 derived values from (62), which attenuate high-frequency content more strongly than the theoretically derived parameter 391 set from the same paper. This behavior produces a closer match to the standard HRF and emphasizes the frequency range 392 considered neuronal in the fMRI literature (63). This yields a simulated hemodynamic response function for each region in 393 the circuit. To facilitate comparison with empirical fMRI data, we apply a 5th-order Butterworth bandpass filter retaining 394 frequencies in the 0.01–0.1 Hz range, consistent with the conventional resting-state fMRI band (63). We verified that replacing 395 the Balloon-Windkessel model with a standard canonical HRF convolution yielded no substantial differences in the resulting 396 timeseries, supporting the robustness of our approach to this modeling choice. 397 J. Hemodynamic Speed, a Time-Varying surrogate for Dopamine Dynamics. Dopamine dynamics from the human fMRI were 398 approximated using a previously validated hemodynamic latency metric (26). For each voxel, relative latency was computed 399 with respect to the global brain signal by identifying the temporal shift that maximized the cross-correlation between the voxel 400 time series and the global signal. To capture task-dependent state changes (e.g., learning vs. learned phases), fMRI time series 401 were segmented into sliding windows comprising five consecutive trials. Latency estimates were computed within each window 402 to characterize dynamic changes across task states. In the original method, hemodynamic latency was inversely associated with 403 putative dopamine signals.
