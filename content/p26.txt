**REVIEW ARTICLE**

Bird’s Eye View of Artificial Intelligence in Neuroscience

Minerva H. Zhou, Emily Lin, Allen Q. Ye, Pratik Mukherjee, and Esther L. Yuh

**Abstract**

This review used a common bibliometric technique to identify areas of application of artificial intelli-

gence (AI) in neuroscience research by analyzing citation patterns. Networks of related publications

were constructed using bibliographic coupling, focusing on peer-reviewed research articles and review

articles that included elements of both AI and neuroscience. The use of AI in neuroscience has experi-

enced significant growth, with a doubling time of 4 to 5 years for AI-related neuroscience publications

in the past decade. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and transformer-based large language models

have been applied across an increasing range of neuroscience subdisciplines, with profound impact on

cognitive, computational, systems, and clinical neuroscience.

**Keywords:** bibliometric,brain–computerinterface,clinicalneurologyandneurosurgery,cognitiveneuroscience,

computational neuroscience, neuroimaging

**Introduction**

This article is a survey of the use of artificial intelligence

(AI) in different areas of neuroscience. The purpose is to

explore the scope of the research literature and the relation-

ships between subdisciplines within it. Unlike a systematic

review, which aims to draw evidence-based conclusions

regarding research questions through critical analysis of a

body of literature compiled by exhaustive search, this over-

view uses citation patterns to characterize growth in the

field, to uncover commonalities across subdisciplines in

the vast field of “neuroscience” that may identify opportu-

nities for interaction and collaboration.[1](#24)

AI and intelligence itself have no strict definitions.[2 ](#24)A

narrower definition of AI might be machines that mimic

or surpass proficiencies generally thought to be in the pur-

view of humans. A broader definition of AI is machines

that can “rapidly find an adequate solution in what

appears a priori (to observers) to be an immense search

space.”[3 ](#24)An even broader definition of AI that has been

articulated,[4 ](#24)and the one that is assumed in this article, is

any work in statistics, machine learning, or other fields

that aims to build such “intelligent” machines.

We identify several trends in publications at the nexus

of AI and neuroscience in the past decade. The AI boom in

the 2010s, driven mostly by computer scientists’ advance-

ments in algorithms such as Convolutional neural networks

(CNNs) and transformers, contributed to a more than five-

fold increase in “AI”-related publications in neuroscience

in the past decade. The effects have been diverse and pro-

found across the neuroscience disciplines. For example, in

cognitive neuroscience, CNNs and transformer-based large

language models have been indispensable in making possi-

ble the real-time “decoding” of neural signals from intra-

cranial implants and from functional magnetic resonance

imaging (fMRI) into fluent speech, images, and verbalized

thoughts. In the study of circuits and systems, CNNs

applied to millions of electron microscopic images were

crucial in enabling the completion of the neuronal “wiring

diagram” of the whole brain of *Drosophila* and a small part

of the visual cortex in the mouse. In computational

**Methods**

We used VOSViewer[5 ](#24)software to visualize patterns

and relationships within research publications related to

both neuroscience and AI. Specifically, we used VOS-

Viewer algorithms to construct networks of publications

and identify “clusters” of related articles, based on bibli-

ographic coupling strength (similarity of articles’ cited

reference lists). The bibliographic coupling strength

between any pair of publications is determined by the

degree of similarity in the two publications’ reference

lists or bibliographies. It has been used to identify publi-

cations likely to share similar concepts.[6 ](#24)Networks

based on bibliographic coupling are like those based on

co-citation. However, the former are based on similarity

of articles’ cited references, while the latter are based on

similarity of the articles’ citing references (i.e., two

publications are “co-cited” if they are both cited by the

same publication).

The clustering algorithm in VOSViewer is unsuper-

vised, and as is common in unsupervised learning, the

user must choose a threshold that determines the reso-

lution (granularity or “fineness”) of the clustering.

Users who are “lumpers” would choose coarser resolu-

tions, resulting in larger clusters of related publica-

tions, while “splitters” would choose finer resolutions,

resulting in a more granular characterization of the

research literature.

Three separate networks were constructed for articles

published in 2016–2018, 2019–2021, and 2022–2024 to

explore the growth of the use of “AI” and related key-

words in neuroscience publications over the past decade.

This approach has limitations (see section “Limitations”)

but provides a rudimentary framework to identify some

of the major areas of research related to both “AI” and

neuroscience.

For each network (covering 2016–2018, 2019–2021,

*or* 2022–2024), articles found via three different types of

searches were combined.

(1) For each of the 3-year periods, all AI-related

articles published in the top 25% impact “neuro”

journals (*n* = 129 journals):

(a) First, Journal Citation Reports (Clarivate, 2025)

was used to identify journals in the categories

Neurosciences, Clinical Neurology, and/or

Neuroimaging.

(b) This list of journals was then narrowed to

those with a 2023 journal impact factor (JIF)

in the top quartile (Q1) of journals (129 of 521

journals) in the same subdiscipline.

(c) Finally, a Web of Science Topic search was used

to identify research or review articles published

within the relevant 3-year period in these Q1

journals with *both* a topic related to neuro-

science AND a topic related to AI, machine

learning or computational neuroscience (Search

Query \#1 in the Supplementary Data S1).

(2) Articles related to both “AI” and “neuro” that

were published in the top 10% impact journals in

the several multidisciplinary science categories

including the top 10% impact journals in the

Medicine, General & Internal and Experimental

Medicine categories (*n* = 92 journals) and 4 of the

top journals in the Psychiatry category (*n* = 4):

(a) First, Journal Citation Reports (Clarivate, 2024)

was used to identify journals in the multidisci-

plinary science and medicine categories listed

above.

(b) This list was then narrowed to those with a

2023 JIF in the top 10% of journals in the cate-

gory (14 of 135 journals).

(c) Journal Citation Reports (Clarivate, 2024) was

used to identify four top journals in the cate-

gory Psychiatry.

(d) Finally, Web of Science Topic Search was used

to identify research and review articles pub-

lished within the relevant 3-year period in

these journals with topics related to both neu-

roscience and AI, machine learning, or com-

putational neuroscience (Search Query \#2 in

the Supplementary Data S1).

(3) Top 1% cited articles related to both “AI” and

“neuro” published in *any* Web of Science (WOS)-

indexed journal:

(a) First, Web of Science “topic” searches were used

to identify articles published within the relevant

3-year period with *both* a topic related to neuro-

science AND a topic related to AI, machine

learning, or computational neuroscience (Search

Query \#3 in the Supplementary Data S1). Thus,

these articles could have been published in *any*

“AI”-related articles published in high-impact clinical

journals indexed in Web of Science have increased by

more than 14-fold and 3-fold, respectively, and a more

than 5-fold increase overall. [Figure 1B and C ](#3)shows the

outcome of the bibliographic coupling network analysis.

In [Figure 1B](#3), similar clusters across the different 3-year

time periods were identified using the same color when

possible. However, as described below, there was signifi-

cant regrouping, merging, recharacterization, and/or divi-

sion of some clusters over time.

For example, in 2022–2024, Cluster 3 (purple)

largely consisted of publications on AI in research on

intracranial tumors, with many related to tumor seg-

2019–2021, Cluster 3 included many publications on

segmentation of other pathology on medical images, in

addition to tumors, using CNNs. Thus, in earlier time

periods, it was a “CNN segmentation” cluster rather

than focusing solely on tumors. This is likely because

tumors in general were among the earliest pathologies

in medical images that were targeted by researchers for

automated segmentation by CNNs.

Additionally, by 2022–2024, fMRI and brain connec-

tivity in the context of cognitive neuroscience had become

a large, very well-defined cluster (Cluster 7, yellow–

green). However, in earlier time periods, it was not well-

defined, and fMRI publications were distributed among

several other clusters including computational neuro-

science and a psychiatry “sub-cluster” within the larger

“clinical applications” cluster (Cluster 4, red).

Another change was the merging of a “circuits and

systems” (dark purple) cluster that appeared in the earlier

time periods (2016–2018 and 2019–2021) into the com-

putational neuroscience cluster (Cluster 1, dark blue) by

the 2022–2024 time period. This may be related to factors

such as the widespread availability of large-scale, high

**Cluster 1 (Dark Blue): Computational**

**Neuroscience, Circuits and Systems**

Publications in this group build and study computational

models of neural processes at the synapse and circuit

level. These models range from detailed simulations of

single neurons to large networks. They are used to study

the brain mechanisms underlying memory, learning,

decision-making, and processing sensory data.

The fields of computational neuroscience and AI are

inextricably linked, and the history of their relationship

has been described.[4](#24),[10](#24)–[16](#24)

Neurobiology was the inspira-

tion for the first theoretical descriptions of ANNs in the

1940s, as well as the development of reinforcement

learning in the 1980s. The first physical implementation

of an ANN, a 5 ton computer occupying an entire room

and known as the Perceptron, in 1958 was touted as the

progenitor to computers that could soon solve nearly any

problem and could even ultimately attain a state of con-

sciousness. This did not materialize, and an “AI winter”

stretched from the early 1970s to 1980. Hopes were

revived in the early 1980s with the demonstration of

some useful “expert systems” in industry that solved

very specific and narrow practical problems, along with

the report of backpropagation in 1986 as a computational

solution to training multilayer ANNs. However, a second

AI winter extended from the late 1980s into the 1990s

when high expectations created earlier in the decade

around expert systems were not met.

By the early 2010s, profound advances in computa-

tional power and data storage capacity had occurred.

This created conditions for the most recent AI “boom,”

driven largely by computer scientists’ exploration and

improvement of algorithms (e.g., CNNs, transformers,

reinforcement learning) based on backpropagation, with

inspiration from neurobiology playing relatively little

role.[16](#24),[17 ](#24)However, it has been proposed that in order to

make significant further progress toward more general

AI, renewed closer interactions between computer sci-

ence and neuroscience will be essential.[4](#24),[16 ](#24)It has also

been suggested that the foundation for general AI will

require first understanding the neural mechanisms underly-

ing the most primitive abilities, such as sensorimotor

abilities, rather than only the highest-level, most recently

evolved human abilities (language, game-playing) ([Fig. 2](#5)).[16](#24)

Continued advances in the past decade in experimental tech-

niques to acquire parallel *in situ* neuronal recordings from

the physical brain at unprecedented spatial and temporal

scale are now available to facilitate and amplify two-way

interactions between computer science and neuroscience

([Fig. 3](#6)).[24](#24)

During the AI winter of the 1990s, computational neuro-

science and AI increasingly diverged, with computational

neuroscientists focused on scaling up biophysical modeling

of the brain’s dynamics. This traditional approach to

quantitative neural modeling began in the 1950s with

the Hodgkin–Huxley differential equations for action

potential propagation[21 ](#24)and progressed toward complex

biophysical models incorporating linear and nonlinear

dynamics among circuits, regions, and/or entire networks

and systems. Modern examples of large-scale neural model-

ing include mean field models,[22 ](#24)such as neural mass mod-

els,[23 ](#24)that can simulate properties of electrophysiological

signals (EEG, Magnetoencephalography \[MEG\], and

ECoG) like oscillatory rhythms in terms of the intrinsic

physiological parameters of their systems of differential

equations and nonlinear activation functions. They are also

used to investigate altered neural dynamics in disease proc-

esses, for example, epilepsy and psychosis.

Although differential equations have been the most

common mathematical approach for describing neural

processes, recurrent neural networks (RNNs) can not only

emulate systems of differential equations but can also be

trained directly on measured data. Due to massively par-

allel neuron recording techniques that became widely

available approximately a decade ago, many studies can

now emphasize iterative feedback between computational

models and experimental data—comparison of the output

of new computational models to new experimental data in

an iterative process of model validation and refinement,

greatly accelerating progress in this area.[24 ](#24)As an exam-

ple, a recent study uses a predictive autoencoder model to

propose a new hypothesis about sequence learning in the

hippocampus and compares directly to experimental neu-

ron recordings in an animal model;[25 ](#24)such models of spik-

ing RNNs are an example of computational models

informing neurobiology rather than only drawing from

them and are revealing the neural mechanisms underlying

working memory.[25](#24),[26 ](#24)Neural population dynamics is

another framework developed to understand how emer-

gent properties arise from large groups of interconnected

neurons. This approach analyzes brain-like RNNs as

dynamical systems and has been successfully used to

model the computations of single local areas in cognitive,

timing, navigation, and motor tasks.[27](#24)–[30](#24)

With the acceleration of AI research over the past dec-

ade, the fields of computational neuroscience and AI

have once more become increasingly linked. Publica-

tions referring to both of these had already formed a dis-

tinct cluster in the 2016–2018 literature ([Fig. 1B](#3)), unlike

some other clusters that emerged more recently. This

cluster of publications grew rapidly in the past decade.

Most publications in this cluster seek to model the brain

mechanisms underlying memory, learning, decision-

making, and processing of sensory data. Examples are

seen in the following sections.

“Reverse engineering” primate vision

By 2012, it was clear that ‘core object recognition’ was

accomplished via a set of serial feedforward computa-

tions, but the form of the computation was unknown.[31](#24)

Fortunately, by that time, electrode arrays had been

developed that could obtain thousands of simultaneous

neural recordings across the visual cortex, ushering in a

new era of investigations of visual system neurophysiol-

ogy at unprecedentedly fine spatial and temporal resolu-

tion.[32 ](#24)In 2014, CNNs trained for object recognition were

shown to predict approximately 50% of the response of

neurons in the inferior temporal cortex, the highest level

of the ventral visual stream ([Figs. 3A ](#6)and [4](#8)). Monkeys

were shown a set of images, and the responses of neurons

in the different hierarchically arranged layers of the ven-

tral visual system were recorded. CNNs were trained to

perform image recognition using the same images.

Remarkably, evaluation of a large number of candidate

CNN models showed that the stronger a CNN model’s

performance at the object recognition task, the more

closely neurons in the CNN’s final layer matched neuro-

nal activity in the inferior temporal cortex.[13](#24),[33](#24),[34 ](#24)Later,

deep neural networks were also shown to model the com-

putations neurons in other areas such as the auditory cor-

tex,[35](#24),[36 ](#24)and language areas in the superior temporal

cortex, parietal lobe and inferior frontal gyrus[37 ](#24)perform

as they process data.

Of note, those involved in this research have pointed out

that despite the successes of these models, it is likely that

the ANNs of today are still relatively simplistic and “do not

yet come close to fully modeling the myriad of processes

contributing to biological visual processing.” Modern

ANNs do not incorporate the complexity of biological neu-

rons’ different morphologies, neurotransmitters, and types

of connections ([Fig. 5](#9)).[15 ](#24)MacPherson et al.[13 ](#24)point out,

“‘\[S\]tatisticians have long stressed this importance of the

distinction between explanatory and predictive model-

ing.’[38 ](#24)Thus, if we intend to use AI systems to model and

possibly inform our understanding of visual processing in

the brain, it is important that we continue to increase the

structural and mechanistic correspondence of AI models to

their counterpart systems within the brain, as well as

strengthening the ability of metrics to measure such

correspondence.”

Recent arguments in support of this distinction between

explanatory and predictive modeling have appeared in the

literature. For example, “brain scores” that quantify models’

ability to predict brain activity and “brain hierarchy scores”

that quantify the hierarchical homology between a model’s

layers and those of the biological brain were reported to

be somewhat diminished for the newest high-performing

CNNs.[14](#24),[39 ](#24)This reversed an earlier trend showing that

Connectome mapping

As described, publications in this group build and study

models of the nervous system, at scales from individual

synapses to large networks of neurons, using mathematical

equations and computer simulations. Accordingly, also

appearing in this cluster are advancements in the efforts

to understand the brain from a “bottom-up” approach—

laying groundwork for understanding neural processes by

mapping the connectome at the synapse level. A promise

of connectome mapping of these organisms is the idea that

understanding the “circuit motifs” underlying the most

primitive abilities, such as sensorimotor integration, that

are common to all organisms, rather than only the highest-

level human abilities such as game-playing, operating

motor vehicles, and language, is essential for further pro-

gress toward more general AI.[4](#24),[16](#24)

In 1986, the full connectome of *Caenorhabditis ele-*

*gans*, constructed through manual annotation of approxi-

mately 8,000 images, had been reported and was shown to

be largely consistent across specimens, with 302 neurons

with simple morphologies and few branches.[41 ](#24)In March

2023, the larva of *Drosophila melanogaster* (3,000 neu-

rons and half a million synapses) was reported, identifying

93 different neuron types and the product of more than

5 years of effort.[40](#24)

In October 2024, publication of the whole-brain con-

nectome of a female adult *D. melanogaster* (140,000

neurons and 50 million synapses), based on approxi-

mately 21 million images[42](#24),[43 ](#24)was a landmark achie-

vement made possible only with advances in AI comput-

ing ([Fig. 7](#12)). Furthermore, in addition to the mapping of

the individual neurons and synapses, a “projectome,” or

map of brain circuits and projections between different

brain regions, providing large-scale information on the

organization of the brain, was included. In this case,

“AI” was not only the target of the research but also

served as an essential practical tool, allowing reconstruc-

tion of neural tissue from electron microscopy images,

which contain gaps and other artifacts that could not

practically be corrected without human oversight.

Regarding mammalian brain connectome mapping,

another milestone in the past year was the reconstruction

of a cubic millimeter of mouse primary visual cortex

(0.2% of the mouse brain) by the Machine Intelligence

from Cortical Networks program.[41](#24),[44](#24),[45](#25),[60 ](#25)Again, CNN

analysis of electron microscopy data was essential in

order to fill in gaps and imperfections. The mapping

included not only neurons, synapses, and glia, but also

mitochondria and blood vessels.
