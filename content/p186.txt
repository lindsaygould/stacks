Abstract

35

Non-oscillatory measures of brain activity such as the spectral slope and Lempel-Ziv complexity are

36

affected by many neurological disorders and modulated by sleep. A multitude of frequency ranges,

37

particularly a broadband (encompassing the full spectrum) and narrowband approach, have been used

38

especially for estimating the spectral slope. However, the effects of choosing different frequency ranges

39

have not yet been explored in detail. Here, we evaluated the impact of sleep stage and task-engagement

40

(resting, attention and memory) on slope and complexity in a narrow- (30 – 45Hz) and broadband (1 –

41

45Hz) frequency range in 28 healthy male human subjects (21.54 ± 1.90 years) using a within-subject

42

design over two weeks with three recording nights and days per subject. We strived to determine how

43

different brain states and frequency ranges affect slope and complexity and how the two measures

44

perform in comparison. In the broadband range, the slope steepened, and complexity decreased

45

continuously from wakefulness to N3 sleep. REM sleep, however, was best discriminated by the

46

narrowband slope. Importantly, slope and complexity also differed between tasks during wakefulness.

47

While the narrowband complexity decreased with task engagement, the slope flattened in both

48

frequency ranges. Interestingly, only the narrowband slope was positively correlated with task

49

performance. Our results show that slope and complexity are sensitive indices of brain state variations

50

during wakefulness and sleep. However, the spectral slope yields more information and could be used

51

for a greater variety of research questions than Lempel-Ziv complexity, especially when a narrowband

52

frequency range is used.

Significance Statement

55

We demonstrate that the spectral slope and Lempel-Ziv complexity differentiate between sleep stages,

56

quiet wakefulness and active tasks, thus making them reliable non-invasive biomarkers of brain states.

57

Critically, these markers were previously assessed in isolation only. Here, we provide evidence that they

58

track highly similar information about the underlying brain state in a broad frequency range (1 – 45Hz).

59

Within this range, slope and complexity distinguish brain states better than in a more narrowband range

60

(30 – 45Hz). However, the slope calculated from the narrowband range is superior in differentiating REM

61

from wakefulness and tracking behavioral performance. Our results demonstrate that the choice of

62

frequency range critically affects the information reflected the spectral slope and Lempel-Ziv complexity.

Introduction
Neural oscillations are a dominant electrophysiological signature of human brain activity. For

67

instance, quiet wakefulness is characterized by pronounced alpha-band activity (Klimesch et al., 1993;

68

Jensen and Mazaheri, 2010) and sleep stages are defined by oscillatory events like sleep spindles and

69

slow oscillations (Terzano et al., 2002; Richard et al., 2012). However, recent evidence suggests that

70

non-oscillatory, irregular brain activity, assessed by Lempel-Ziv complexity (Lempel and Ziv, 1976 and

71

Welch, 1984) or the spectral slope (He, 2014), also carries meaningful information about

72

electrophysiological variations across brain states.

66

The spectral slope is obtained in the frequency domain and reflects the steepness of the power

74

spectrum. In contrast, Lempel-Ziv complexity (Welch, 1984) is computed in the time domain and reflects

75

the regularity of a signal (Lau et al., 2022). Thus, Lempel-Ziv complexity is still strongly influenced by

76

oscillatory activity (see Figure 1 – 4), whereas the spectral slope captures mainly aperiodic activity

77

(Donoghue et al., 2020).

Computational modelling has demonstrated that the spectral slope constitutes a marker of the

79

brain’s excitation to inhibition (E/I) balance (Gao et al., 2017), which is impaired in a variety of clinical

80

conditions (Rubenstein and Merzenich, 2003; Gao and Penzes, 2015; Robertson et al., 2019; Karalunas

81

et al., 2022). Interestingly, some disorders as for instance epilepsy have been associated not only with

82

alterations in the spectral slope but also in complexity (Wong, 2010; Aarabi and He, 2012; Zhu et al.,

83

2017). Overall, previous studies suggest that both, spectral slope and Lempel-Ziv complexity capture

84

brain state changes in similar ways. The slope steepens (i.e., decreases) under anesthesia (Gao et al.,

2017; Colombo et al., 2019; Lendner et al., 2020; Waschke et al., 2021) and during sleep (Ma et al.,

2016; Schartner et al., 2017). Evidence from Waschke et al. (2021) suggests that the spectral slope

94

A big issue, however, that has hampered the assessment of and comparability between slope

95

and complexity is the huge heterogeneity in frequency ranges used to calculate these measures. While

there will be no frequency range without potential confounds, it is important to compare different

97

frequency ranges and calculation settings to gain a better understanding of how the choice of a certain

98

frequency range affects the data. Particularly for the spectral slope, researchers have argued either in

99

favor of broadband (Podvalny et al., 2015; Waschke et al., 2021; Karalunas et al., 2022) or more

100

narrowband (Gao et al., 2017; Lendner et al., 2020) frequency ranges, commonly within 1 – 45/50Hz.

101

While broadband ranges (e.g., 1 – 45Hz) encompass more of the total signal and result in better overall

102

fits (Donoghue et al., 2020; Gerster et al., 2022), narrowband ranges (e.g., 30 – 45Hz) are less affected

103

by slower oscillatory activity and reflect mostly aperiodic activity (Gao et al., 2017; Lendner et al., 2020).

104

But also regarding complexity, recent evidence demonstrated that this metric is strongly affected by

105

different frequency contents and might be mainly driven by lower frequencies (González et al., 2022).

106

Taken together, a functional overlap between the spectral slope and Lempel-Ziv complexity is

107

suggested in the literature. However, direct comparisons between the two measures are rare and limited

108

to quiet wakefulness and anesthesia (Medel et al., 2023). Thus, even though slope and complexity are

109

both biomarkers of arousal that are similarly modulated by changes in brain states, it is yet unclear how

110

they behave in comparison. Moreover, it remains unclear how the selection of different frequency ranges

111

affects the information captured by them.

Participants and inclusion criteria

121

We recorded data from 28 biologically male participants (18 – 25 years; mean age 21.54 ± 1.90

122

years) to avoid previously reported sex effects on sleep, attributed to hormonal variations (Alonso et al.,

2021; Plamberger et al., 2021). Final sample sizes varied for each analysis between N = 26 – 28 as

124

some participants had missing data for specific tasks or timepoints (the exact sample size for each

125

analysis is provided in the corresponding figure caption). All participants were free of medication and

126

did not suffer from a mental or physiological illness or reported sleep problems. They adhered to a

127

regular sleep-wake rhythm (i.e., regular bedtimes with about 8 hours of sleep per night) and refrained

128

from drug use and above-average caffeine consumption (more than three cups of coffee per day) during

129

participation. For screening purposes, all subjects filled in an entrance questionnaire in which we

130

checked for sleep quality, mood, anxiety, perceived stress level and chronotype to exclude individuals

131

with clinical symptoms in advance (cf., Figure 1 – 1) . Written informed consent was obtained from every

132

participant and they were remunerated with either 100€ and 16 hours course credit or 50€ and 24 hours

133

course credit. The study was approved by the local ethics committee and conducted in agreement with

134

the Declaration of Helsinki.

Declarative memory task

178

Participants encoded a set of 80 word pairs on days 7, 10 and 13. To avoid learning effects over

179

time, a different but similarly difficult set of 80 word pairs was presented on each of the three days. The

180

order of the sets was randomized across subjects. Each set was presented twice for 14min during

encoding and the data from both encoding sessions was pooled. Each word pair was presented for

182

1500ms, followed by a fixation-cross for 8500ms. Participants were instructed to encode the word pair

183

as vividly as possible during the presentation of the fixation-cross by imagining a semantic connection

184

between the two words. During the cued recall sessions, only the first word of a pair was presented, and

185

participants were asked to press a button on the response time box as soon as they remembered the

186

second word. Whenever a button was pressed, the participant was instructed to name the missing word

187

and a fixation-cross appeared for 3500ms while the experimenter noted the answer. When no button

188

was pressed, the fixation-cross appeared automatically after 6500ms. Recall performance was

189

measured as the percentage of correctly recalled word pairs during each retrieval session. To assess

190

the overnight change in performance, the increase in percentage from the evening performance to the

191

following morning was computed.

EEG preprocessing
In a first step, the raw data were processed with the BrainVision Analyzer software, and we

213

applied a 0.3Hz high-pass as well as a 50Hz notch filter. EEG channels were re-referenced to linked

214

mastoids and the online reference Cz was restored. We corrected for eye movements with the Gratton

215

& Coles method (Gratton et al., 1983; only implemented for data during wakefulness) and ran an

216

automatic artifact detection procedure on all scalp EEG channels, which was manually checked

217

afterwards. Events with a voltage jump exceeding 50μV/ms, an absolute voltage difference of more than

218

400μV within 200ms or activity lower than 0.5μV for at least 100ms were marked as bad intervals. If

219

severe muscle or movement artefacts were missed, they were additionally marked manually. The data

220

were then down-sampled to 250Hz and exported for further analyses in MATLAB. The continuous data

221

were subsequently segmented into epochs of 4s for each task and sleep stage using the fieldtrip toolbox

222

(Oostenveld et al., 2011). To be able to compare all task- and sleep-data, we decided to set the epoch-

223

length to 4s as this enabled the best tradeoff between sufficient epochs even for the shortest tasks (3min

224

resting sessions) and an adequate frequency resolution. All artifact-containing epochs (defined as > 1%

225

being detected as artifact) were removed. Since the remaining number of clean epochs from the tasks

226

(resting, Go/Nogo, encoding and retrieval) and sleep-stages (WAKE, N1, N2, N3 and REM) varied due

227

to different recording lengths, we balanced the number of epochs across tasks and sleep-stages for the

228

multivariate pattern analyses (MVPA). We set the maximum number of epochs for the MVPA analyses

Spectral Slope

234

To obtain the spectral slope, we first calculated power-spectra between 0.5 – 45Hz from the

235

preprocessed, 4s segmented data via the mtmfft method in Fieldtrip (Oostenveld et al., 2011) using a

236

multi-taper approach (1Hz frequency smoothing). To extract the slope, we applied robust linear fits

237

(using the robust fit MATLAB function) in log-log space between 30 – 45Hz. We decided to use robust

linear fits instead of using the FOOOF algorithm (Donoghue et al., 2020) for the narrowband frequency

239

range since this approach has already been established to yield a sensitive aperiodic marker of arousal

240

by Lendner et al. (2020) and because in this frequency range also the FOOOF would approximate a

241

linear fit, thus leading to highly comparable results. However, for the broadband frequency range (1 –

242

45Hz), we applied the FOOOF algorithm to extract the slope since linear fits would have been skewed

243

by oscillatory bumps in the power spectrum.

Lempel-Ziv Complexity

cr
ip
t

238

We followed previous approaches (Schartner et al., 2015; Mediano et al., 2021; Medel et al.,

246

2023) and calculated the Lempel-Ziv-Welch complexity (Lempel and Ziv, 1976; Welch, 1984) per

247

channel and epoch. To obtain the complexity in the same frequency ranges in which we calculated the

248

spectral slope, we applied additional 1Hz or 30Hz high-pass and 45Hz low-pass filters. As Rivolta et al.

249

(2014) demonstrated that 1000 datapoints are sufficient for reliable Lempel-Ziv complexity analyses

250

during sleep, we used the same 4s segmented data (corresponding to 1000 sampling points per epoch

251

in our down sampled data) as for the spectral slope. We then applied a Hilbert-transformation on each

252

epoch to obtain the instantaneous amplitude. Afterwards, we binarized the resulting single epoch data

253

around its median amplitude and transformed it into a binary sequence. Values of 1 were given for

254

amplitude samples above the median and values of 0 for amplitudes below (or equal with) the median.

255

This binary sequence of ones and zeros was subjected to the Lempel-Ziv-Welch complexity algorithm

256

(Comsa, 2019) in MATLAB. In general, higher complexity values (normalized between 0 and 1) reflect

257

more random and unpredictable signals (cf., Figure 1 – 4).

ANOVA-type analyses and correlations

263

All analyses involved three repeated measurements (days 7, 10 and 13; cf., Figure 1) and

264

therefore at least two factors (lab-session and task or sleep stage). Since in most cases at least one

265

assumption for parametrical testing was violated, we decided to compute more conservative semi-

266

parametrical analyses with the MANOVA.RM package (Friedrich et al., 2019). For these factorial

267

analyses, data were averaged over all EEG electrodes to facilitate interpretation of the results. In the

268

statistical results, we always refer to the Wald-Type-Statistics (WTS) with empirical p-values obtained

269

from permutation resampling procedures and 10.000 iterations. Whenever multiple comparisons were

270

conducted, p-values were corrected with the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure (Benjamini and Hochberg,

271

1995).
For correlational analyses, we computed Spearman rho coefficients instead of Pearson

273

correlations whenever the normality assumption was significantly violated (indicated by Shapiro-Wilk

274

tests) and for all cluster correlations on the whole scalp level. For the cluster corrected correlation

275

approach, we used the Monte-Carlo method with 10.000 iterations to assess the relationship between

276

EEG parameters per channel and behavioral measures.

Discussion

In this study comprising three experimental recordings with multiple measurements per subject,

we demonstrated that the spectral slope and Lempel-Ziv complexity (I) reliably delineate sleep stages

404

and (II) are modulated by different cognitive tasks. Critically, we provided evidence that (III) the

405

modulation of the slope and complexity strongly depends on the frequency content. While the broadband

406

(1 – 45Hz) slope and complexity were more strongly modulated by sleep stages in general, the

407

narrowband (30 – 45Hz) slope best differentiated REM sleep from wakefulness and reflects mainly

408

aperiodic activity. Moreover, we found that (IV) active task engagement (i.e., switching from resting to

409

an attention or learning task) was associated with flatter slopes in the narrow- and broadband range,

410

but only with higher complexity in the broadband range. The broadband range was also better suited to

411

capture differences between tasks in the classification analyses. However, (V) only the narrowband

412

slope tracked task performance in an auditory attention task and trended towards significance regarding

413

memory performance.

414

The narrowband slope uniquely tracks aperiodic brain activity in REM
Our findings corroborate previous research demonstrating that the spectral slope and Lempel-

416

Ziv complexity are sensitive markers of sleep stage (Abásolo et al., 2015; Schartner et al., 2017; Lendner

417

et al., 2020; Bódizs et al., 2021; Pascovich et al., 2022; Kozhemiako et al., 2022). We extended these

418

findings by leveraging repeated EEG recordings per subject and confirmed that the two parameters can

419

robustly differentiate between all sleep stages and wakefulness across multiple recording days. In

420

contrast to complexity, the calculation of the slope was about 3.5 times more computationally efficient

421

with our implementations, highlighting its practical applicability for closed loop and clinical settings.

Limitations

In this study, we focused exclusively on two specific EEG derivates: spectral slope and Lempel-

493

Ziv complexity. Consequently, we did not compare these measures against other established analytical

494

techniques such as spectral power, entropy, or network analyses (e.g., coherence). However, regarding

495

spectral power, previous research demonstrated that the spectral slope is superior in discriminating

496

REM sleep from wakefulness compared to slow oscillation or gamma power, and performs similarly to

497

beta power (Lendner et al., 2020; Kozhemiako et al., 2022). Additionally, Biggs et al. (2022) showed

498

that Lempel-Ziv complexity also performed similarly well in comparison to alpha power and outperformed

499

permutation entropy in capturing age-related changes in brain activity induced by anesthesia. However,

500

comparisons with other biomarkers, such as heart rate variability or blood pressure (Radha et al., 2019;

501

Mitsukura et al., 2020; Kuula and Pesonen, 2021), as well as specific benchmark tests involving both

502

spectral slope and Lempel-Ziv complexity, are lacking and merit exploration in future large datasets.
It should also be noted that the tasks in this study were not specifically designed for the analysis

504

of varying levels of task demand or difficulty as the dataset was originally designed for other purposes.

505

Even though the participants reported differences in task difficulty, other significant factors that could

506

potentially influence the results include differences in task modality. While there is evidence that

507

attentional and learning tasks do differ regarding their level of cognitive demand (Sweller, 2011;

508

Bambrah et al., 2019), it also seems to be dependent on the specific task instructions and modalities. In

509

the future, it might be promising to contrast tasks that exclusively rely on different cognitive resources

510

and sensory modalities.

Conclusions

523

Our results demonstrate that EEG spectral slope and Lempel-Ziv complexity are powerful

524

indices of brain states during sleep and wakefulness. We present robust evidence from multiple

525

recordings of three within-subject measurements, revealing that sleep stages and various cognitive

526

tasks are reliably indexed by both spectral slope and Lempel-Ziv complexity. When derived from the full

527

spectrum, slope and complexity capture redundant information and are influenced by oscillatory activity,

528

particularly during sleep. In contrast, the narrowband slope (30 – 45Hz) mainly indexes aperiodic brain

529

activity, offering additional insights into underlying brain states. It provides a means to infer potential

530

changes in the excitation/inhibition balance using only scalp electrodes, thus characterizing the spectral

531

slope as a unique electrophysiological marker and distinguishing it from other measures such as spectral

532

power. Moreover, recent evidence has shown that aperiodic activity on the scalp level can even offer

533

insights into excitability changes on a cellular basis and reflects variations in pyramidal cell calcium

534

activity (Lendner et al., 2023).
In the present study, the narrowband slope also proved to be the most powerful index of

536

behavioral performance and was best suited to differentiate REM sleep from wakefulness and all other

537

sleep stages without the additional use of EOG or EMG. Computing complexity in the narrowband range,

538

however, did not yield any particularly meaningful results and should be undertaken with caution.

539

Therefore, considering the faster computation time and the more diverse range of applications when

540

using different frequency ranges as well as its correlation with task performance, the slope might be

541

more practical and useful in most circumstances compared to complexity. Taken together, our study

542

emphasizes the importance of considering the parametrization of brain activity into full, oscillatory and

543

aperiodic components to comprehensively understand the dynamics underlying passive and active brain

544

states during sleep and wakefulness.
