A B S T R A C T

Background and objective: Repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS) is a safe and effective treatment for major depressive disorder (MDD); however, this treatment currently lacks reliable biomarkers of treatment response. TMS-evoked potentials (TEPs), measured using TMS-electroencephalography (TMS-EEG), have been suggested as potential biomarker candidates, with the N100 peak being one of the most promising. This study investigated the association between baseline N100 amplitude and 1 Hz right dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (R- DLPFC) accelerated rTMS (arTMS) treatment in MDD. Methods: Baseline TMS-EEG sessions were performed for 23 MDD patients. All patients then underwent 40 ses- sions of 1 Hz R-DLPFC (F4) arTMS over 5 days and a follow-up TMS-EEG session one week after the end of theses arTMS sessions. Results: Baseline N100 amplitude at F4 showed a strong positive association (p \< .001) with treatment outcome. The association between the change in N100 amplitude (baseline to follow-up) and treatment outcome did not remain significant after Bonferroni correction (p = .06, corrected; p = .03, uncorrected). Furthermore, treatment responders had a significantly larger mean baseline F4 TEP amplitude during the N100 time frame compared to non-responders (p \< .001). Topographically, after Bonferroni correction, F4 is the only electrode at which its baseline N100 amplitude showed a significant positive association (p \< .001) with treatment outcome. Limitations: Lack of control group and auditory masking. Conclusion: Baseline N100 amplitude showed a strong association with treatment outcome and thus demon- strated great potential to be utilized as a cost-effective and widely adoptable biomarker of rTMS treatment in MDD.

2.4. Clinical outcome

3.1. Demographics

Clinical treatment outcome was measured using percent improve- ment in Beck Depression Inventory-II (BDI-II) (Beck et al., 1961) scores, which was calculated using the baseline and second follow-up (four weeks after the arTMS treatment session) BDI-II scores.

23 participants, 13 female and 10 male, between the ages of 26–61, were included in the study. The mean age in years was 41.8 ± 10.6 SD. Full demographic and clinical characteristics are given in Table 1. Average baseline BDI-II was 34.0 ± 9.3. The response rate 4 weeks after treatment was 39.1 %. segment includes 180 pulses, which exceeds the number of total pulses administered in previous studies (Rogasch et al., 2014; Sun et al., 2016). Preprocessing steps were based on the previously published methods by Rogasch et al. (Rogasch et al., 2014). To remove artefacts generated by the rTMS pulse, all data from 10 ms before the pulse to 6 ms after the pulse were excluded from the analysis. A zero-phase FIR 1 Hz highpass filter and a zero-phase 60 Hz notch filter were applied. Data was epoched from 300 ms pre-pulse to 500 ms post-pulse. Examination for bad trials and channels was done manually to remove data with clearly visible contaminants such as noisy electrodes and patient movements; bad trials were removed while bad channels were interpolated. Two rounds of fast independent component analysis (FastICAs) were per- formed to remove artefactual components according to features pre- sented in Rogasch et al. (Rogasch et al., 2014). The first round was used to remove remaining rTMS pulse artefacts, after which a zero-phase FIR 80 Hz lowpass filter was applied, then a second round of ICA was used to remove other artefacts including decay artefacts, eye blinks, eye movements, muscle artefacts, auditory and noise-related artefacts, and electrode artefacts. Lastly, data was baseline corrected to the period from 300 to 100 ms before the onset of the rTMS pulse and re-referenced to the all-channel average.

TEP was obtained for each electrode by averaging all trials. N100 amplitude was determined by finding the most negative peak between 80 and 130 ms after the rTMS pulse onset.

Our results, which were obtained through practical EEG and rTMS methods that do not require particularly extensive apparatus to be added to conventional clinical setups, are in line with previous reports and thus add to the growing body of evidence supporting the potential of utilizing baseline TEPs as biomarkers of rTMS treatment outcome in MDD. First and foremost, the baseline F4 N100 amplitude had a strong positive associ- ation with BDI percent improvement (Fig. 1). Second, prior to Bonfer- roni correction, the change in F4 N100 amplitude from baseline to follow-up had a weak positive association with treatment outcome (Fig. 2). Also, the mean F4 N100 amplitude decreased after arTMS treatment, but this decrease was not statistically significant (Supple- mentary Fig. 3). Moreover, the mean baseline F4 TEP amplitude during the N100 time frame was found to be significantly more negative for treatment responders than non-responders (Fig. 3). Lastly, prior to Bonferroni correction, baseline N100 amplitudes at the F3 electrode, representing the left-DLPFC (L-DLPFC) region, and F4 electrode, repre- senting the R-DLPFC region, were found to be significantly associated with treatment outcome. Baseline N100 amplitudes at other electrodes had no statistically significant association with treatment outcome (Fig. 4).

The strong positive association between baseline F4 N100 amplitude and treatment outcome (Fig. 1), which suggests patients with larger F4 N100 amplitude at baseline led to larger improvements in MDD symp- toms post 1 Hz arTMS, is in line with results by Sun and colleagues, who utilized MST as the treatment instead of 1 Hz rTMS and used suicidality scores instead of BDI scores; this association was highly significant in both studies (p \< .001) (Sun et al., 2016).
