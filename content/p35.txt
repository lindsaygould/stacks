ABSTRACT

22

Theta (4–10 Hz) oscillations seem well-suited for coordinating neural activity. Many studies

23

have focused on theta’s role in long-range coordination across brain regions (e.g., connectivity

24

between the prefrontal cortex and the hippocampus). It remains unclear how theta coordinates

25

neural activity more locally within prefrontal subareas. We examined neural activity in three

26

frontal areas (i.e., dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (dlPFC), ventrolateral prefrontal cortex (vlPFC),

27

and frontal eye fields (FEF)) as non-human primates categorized dot patterns. We found that

28

theta flexibly coordinated spiking activity and higher-frequency oscillations within and between

29

frontal areas. First, theta phase in all areas was coupled to spiking information in the FEF and

30

seemed to gate information that was behaviorally relevant. Second, theta influences were

31

routed in opposite directions depending on feedback. Theta flowed in a posterior direction to the

32

FEF during choices and after correct outcomes. Theta influences reversed directionality and

33

flowed in an anterior direction after incorrect outcomes. Third, theta organized nested cross-

34

frequency, phase-amplitude interactions. Theta was coupled to beta (15-30Hz) oscillations, both

35

within and between areas. Beta, in turn, was coupled to gamma (40-90Hz) oscillations, but

36

mainly locally. Together, our results position theta as a critical mechanism that flexibly and

37

dynamically coordinates neural activity within and across the frontal cortex.

INTRODUCTION

49

Cognition is not static. The brain must dynamically shift how information is coordinated and

50

updated within and between regions. This flexibility seems to be achieved, in part, by neural

51

oscillations (Buzsáki & Draguhn, 2004; Engel, Fries, & Singer, 2001; Fries, 2015). Oscillations

52

organize neural activity within local circuits (Lei et al., 2026; Cardin et al., 2009; Lundqvist et al.,

53

2016) and across larger distances (Gregoriou, Gotts, Zhou, & Desimone, 2009; Buschman &

54

Miller, 2007; von Stein & Sarnthein, 2000).

55
56

Theta (4–10 Hz) oscillations, in particular, seem to be well suited for coordinating neural activity.

57

Theta oscillations synchronize regions across long-range distances (Siapas et al., 2005; Jones

58

& Wilson, 2005; Fujisawa & Buzsáki 2011; Liebe et al., 2012). Theta organizes into large-scale

59

traveling waves across the cortex (Zhang, Watrous, Patel, & Jacobs, 2018; Patel et al., 2012).

60

And theta routes information selectively through distinct pathways (Colgin et al., 2009; Colgin,

61

2013; Hasselmo et al., 2002; Helfrich et al., 2018; Fiebelkorn & Kastner, 2019). Within the

62

prefrontal cortex, theta oscillations tend to increase when cognitive control is needed

63

(Womelsdorf, Vinck, Leung, & Everling, 2010; Han et al., 2026). Theta is observed during

64

surprising events, such as negative feedback (Cavanagh, Frank, Klein, & Allen, 2010; Trujillo &

65

Allen, 2007; van de Vijver et al., 2011; Andreou et al., 2017; Brincat & Miller, 2015; Loonis et al.,

66

2017) and stimulus conflict (Muralidharan et al., 2023; Choi et al., 2024). Furthermore, theta

67

synchrony peaks around “decision-points” (Cavanagh & Frank, 2014) to integrate information

68

from multiple sources (e.g., task rules, stimulus-reward associations, and reward expectations;

69

Womelsdorf, Johnston, Vinck, & Everling, 2010; Benchenane et al., 2010; van Wingerden et al.,

70

2010).

71
72

The majority of these studies have focused on a single role for theta in isolation. Here, we

73

address whether theta can dynamically shift its role according to changing task demands, thus

74

supporting the flexibility associated with cognitive function. Additionally, the coordinating role of

75

theta in the prefrontal cortex has largely focused on long-range connectivity (e.g., with

76

hippocampus or visual cortex). It is generally underexplored how theta coordinates processing

77

within the frontal cortex. Thus, we examined neural activity from multiple frontal areas,

78

simultaneously.

79

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.03.729810; this version posted June 4, 2026. The copyright holder for this preprint
(which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission.

80

We examined the neural dynamics as non-human primates (NHPs) categorized dot patterns.

81

Neural activity was recorded in three frontal areas: the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (dlPFC),

82

the ventrolateral prefrontal cortex (vlPFC), and the frontal eye fields (FEF). We found that the

83

FEF emerged as a critical hub during decision-making and feedback processing. Theta phase,

84

in all areas, was selectively coupled to FEF spiking information. The type of information coupled

85

to theta shifted across the trial and depended on what was immediately relevant. This was

86

accompanied with a change in the directionality of theta causality. Theta influences flowed in the

87

posterior direction toward the FEF during choices and after correct outcomes. By contrast, theta

88

influences flowed in the anterior direction away from the FEF after incorrect outcomes. Phase-

89

amplitude coupling revealed that theta phase organized higher-frequency oscillations. Theta

90

was coupled to beta oscillations, within and across areas. Beta, in turn, was coupled to gamma

91

oscillation, more locally.

RESULTS

94

Non-human primates (NHPs) learned to categorize dot patterns (Fig. 1A; Loonis et al., 2017;

95

Posner & Keele, 1968). Two new dot patterns were generated before each session and

96

functioned as the category prototypes (see Methods). The NHPs never saw these prototypes.

97

Instead, they were shown category exemplars generated by randomly distorting the dot

98

positions of the prototypes. During each trial, a category exemplar appeared at the center of the

99

screen (Fig. 1B; sample epoch). After a memory delay (delay epoch), two new exemplars were

100

shown on the left and right sides of the screen (choice epoch). One of the choice exemplars

101

came from the same category as the sample exemplar presented earlier (i.e., its category

102

“match”). The other exemplar came from the other category (category “non-match”). The NHPs

103

were trained to fixate on the “match” exemplar to receive a reward (correct choice). Visual

104

feedback was given simultaneously with the reward (outcome). After correct choices, the color

105

of the chosen exemplar turned from white to green. After incorrect choices, the color of the

106

chosen exemplar turned red, and a “timeout” was given before the start of the next trial.

107
108

Local field potentials (LFPs) and spiking activity were recorded from three electrode arrays

109

positioned in the frontal cortex (Fig 1C). Two arrays were centered in more anterior locations in

110

the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (dlPFC) and the ventrolateral prefrontal cortex (vlPFC). The

111

third array was centered in a more posterior location in the frontal eye fields (FEF). The current

112

analysis only included sessions in which the NHPs successfully learned the categories (see

113

Methods; 66 out of 76 total sessions; average number of trials: 777.48 ± 219.21 SD; average

Theta organized nested cross-frequency interactions

291

DISCUSSION

377

Our results characterized how theta coordinated neural activity within and across frontal areas

378

as NHPs made choices and received feedback. First, theta phase was selectively coupled to

379

spiking information in the FEF. When the NHPs made behavioral responses, choice information

380

in FEF spiking was coupled to LFP theta phase, in all areas. Then after receiving feedback,

381

outcome information (and not choice information) became coupled to theta phase, in all areas.

382

Second, theta influences were routed in opposite directions depending on feedback. During

383

behavioral choices and following correct outcomes, theta influences flowed in the posterior

384

direction from the dlPFC/vlPFC to the FEF. Following incorrect outcomes, theta influences

385

reversed and flowed in the anterior direction from the FEF to the dlPFC/vlPFC. Third, theta

386

organized nested cross-frequency, phase-amplitude interactions. Theta phase was coupled to

387

beta amplitude (theta-beta PAC), and beta phase was coupled to gamma amplitude (beta-

388

gamma PAC). Theta-beta PAC was strong during choices and after incorrect outcomes, and

389

beta-gamma PAC was strong after correct outcomes. Together, our results provide a multi-level

390

account of how theta dynamically coordinates activity in the frontal cortex.

391
392

Theta seemed to function as a gating mechanism to select relevant spiking information. Many

393

reports have shown that theta phase couples to spiking activity (O’Keefe & Recce, 1993;

394

Rutishauser et al., 2010; Reddy et al., 2021; Benchenane et al., 2010; Han et al., 2026). Fewer

395

studies have shown that spiking information, as opposed to spike rates, also couples to LFP

396

phase (Voloh et al., 2020; Kayser et al., 2009). Our results extend this literature by showing that

397

the type of information coupled to theta phase depended on what was immediately relevant.

398

During the choice and outcome epochs, spiking activity carried information about multiple task

399

variables simultaneously, as they often do (Rigotti et al., 2013; Mante et al., 2013; Cromer et al.,

400

2010). However, only currently relevant information was coupled to theta phase: choice

401

information during choices, and outcome information following feedback. Theta phase thus

402

seemed to organize which information was available. This provides a mechanism by which

403

information can be dynamically reconfigured during changing task demands.

404
405

This gating mechanism may depend on an interaction of excitation and inhibition. The optimal

406

phase of decoding spiking information was offset from the peak phase of spike-field coupling.

407

This offset is consistent with previous reports (Kayser et al., 2009; Voloh et al., 2020; Schaefer

408

et al., 2006) and helps rule out the possibility that information decoding was higher simply

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.03.729810; this version posted June 4, 2026. The copyright holder for this preprint
(which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission.

409

because spiking activity was high. Furthermore, the timing hints at a more complex interaction

410

between excitation and inhibition. At the peak of theta, overall spike rates increased. Then

411

during the falling phases of theta, activity waned, but information increased. This is suggestive

412

of inhibitory mechanisms (e.g., interneurons; Rao et al., 2000; Lewis et al., 2012) that “shape”

413

information from overall activation. Indeed, beta amplitude, which has been implicated in

414

inhibitory control (Lundqvist et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2026), also peaked during the falling

415

phases of theta.

416
417

The FEF emerged as a dynamic hub. During behavioral choices, theta influences flowed into

418

the FEF from the dlPFC and the vlPFC. This replicates previous studies (Babapoor-Farrokhran

419

et al., 2017) and is consistent with a role of the FEF in relaying top-down information from the

420

prefrontal cortex to the visual cortex in order to direct spatial attention (Moore & Armstrong,

421

2003; Moore & Fallah, 2001; Schafer & Moore, 2007). These results are also consistent with a

422

more recent study showing that FEF theta is critical for the readout of working memory content

423

(Han et al., 2026). However, after incorrect choices, theta influences reversed directionality and

424

flowed anteriorly back to the dlPFC/vlPFC. This reversal suggests that the FEF functioned as a

425

critical node in the evaluation of choices and outcomes (Teichert, Yu, & Ferrera, 2014; Shteyn &

426

Olson, 2024; Ding & Hikosaka, 2006) and was not a simple relay of top-down information.

427

Furthermore, only FEF spiking information was coupled to theta phase. Spiking activity in the

428

dlPFC/vlPFC carried choice and outcome information, but these were not coupled to LFP

429

phase. Together, these results suggest that the FEF played a privileged role in selecting and

430

broadcasting choices and errors.

431
432

Our observations of theta-beta PAC and beta-gamma PAC were unexpected, as many studies

433

have focused only on coupling between theta phase and gamma amplitude (Tort et al., 2010;

434

Lisman & Jensen, 2013; Belluscio et al., 2012; Saint Amour di Chanaz et al., 2023; Canolty et

435

al., 2006; Tamura et al., 2017; Spyropoulos, Bosman, & Fries, 2018; Bonnefond, Kastner, &

436

Jensen, 2017; Voloh et al., 2015; Lisman & Idiart, 1995). In our study, theta-gamma PAC was

437

present, but did not reliably change across trial epochs or by trial outcome. This suggests that

438

theta had a more indirect influence on gamma, via beta (Bastos et al., 2015; Lundqvist et al.,

439

2016; Lundqvist et al., 2018). In fact, theta-beta and beta-gamma PAC were anticorrelated and

440

seemed to reflect opposing network configurations. For theta-beta PAC, the phase-amplitude

441

relationship was consistent for within-area and between-area comparisons, suggesting that

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.03.729810; this version posted June 4, 2026. The copyright holder for this preprint
(which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission.

442

theta-beta PAC may be more important for more long-distance routing. By contrast, for beta-

443

gamma PAC, the phase-amplitude relationship was only consistent for within-area comparisons,

444

suggesting that beta-gamma PAC may be more important for local coupling. Beta, as the

445

intermediate frequency, may mediate between the long-distance routing of theta-beta PAC and

446

the local maintenance of beta-gamma PAC. This would be consistent with beta’s role in top-

447

down control (Buschman & Miller, 2007; Bastos et al., 2015; Lundqvist et al., 2024; Spitzer &

448

Haegens, 2017).

449
450

Taken together, our results suggest that theta functions as a “master clock” that coordinates

451

network dynamics in the frontal cortex. This coordination occurred at multiple levels. Theta

452

gated spiking information, routed influences between subareas, and organized higher-frequency

453

oscillations. For each of these mechanisms, theta’s role was not static across the trial, but

454

shifted dramatically from choices to outcomes. Together, these findings emphasize theta’s role

455

in governing flexible cognition.

MATERIAL & METHODS

457

Task procedures

458

All task procedures have been previously described (Loonis et al., 2017). Briefly, non-human

459

primates (NHPs; two adult rhesus macaques; NHP 1: female, 6 years, 6.5 kg; NHP 2: male, 9

460

years, 13 kg) learned to categorize dot patterns. Novel category prototypes were generated

461

before each session. These prototypes were designed such that task difficulty was roughly

462

equivalent across sessions (see Loonis et al., 2017 for details). Category exemplars were

463

created by distorting the dot positions of the prototype (Posner & Keele, 1968; Distortion level

464

1). The number of category exemplars increased progressively as the NHPs learned the

465

categories. All procedures followed the guidelines according to the Massachusetts Institute of

466

Technology Committee on Animal Care and the National Institutes of Health.

467
468

The NHPs started each trial by fixating on a central fixation point (2.5 visual degrees) for 0.5

469

seconds (Fig. 1B; fixation epoch). Then, a category exemplar (7 visual degrees x 7 visual

470

degrees) was presented at the center of the screen for 1.0 second (sample epoch). After a

471

variable working memory delay (delay epoch; 0.85 seconds to 1.25 seconds), two new

472

exemplars were presented on the left and right sides of the screen (choice; 9 visual degrees

473

from the center). One choice exemplar came from the same category as the sample exemplar

474

(i.e., its “match” category). The other exemplar came from the other, “non-match” category. The

475

NHPs were trained to fixate on the match exemplar for at least 0.7 seconds. Visual feedback

476

was given after each choice. After correct choices, the chosen exemplar turned from white to

477

green, and the NHPs received a juice reward (simultaneously). After incorrect choices, the

478

chosen exemplar turned red, juice was withheld, and the NHPs were given a “timeout” (5–16

479

seconds) before the start of the next trial.

480
481

The NHPs completed multiple blocks of trials during each session. The number of unique

482

category exemplars increased exponentially across blocks (i.e., 2block number of exemplars).

483

The NHPs progressed to the next block after reaching a learning criterion (i.e., >80% accuracy

484

on the previous twenty trials and >70% accuracy on the previous ten trials for each of the four

485

main conditions: Category ‘A’ on the left, Category ‘A’ on the right, Category ‘B’ on the left,

486

Category ‘B’ on the right). For the current analysis, we excluded sessions in which the NHPs did

487

not reach at least training block six (i.e., 10 sessions were excluded out of 76 total sessions).

488

We only analyzed trials during and after training block three, to focus on post-learning neural

489

coding.

Granger Causality

558

Directional influences between LFPs across areas were measured using spectral Granger

559

causality (sGC; Geweke, 1982). Like traditional Granger causality, sGC quantifies how variance

560

in one time-series can be explained by the past values of another time-series, beyond the

561

variance that can be explained using that time-series’s own past values. Unlike traditional

562

Granger causality, sGC expresses these influences in the spectral (frequency) domain and does

563

not rely on fitting a specific autoregressive model. We computed sGC between each pair of

564

between-area electrodes in 500 ms non-overlapping time windows. Directionality was assessed

565

by comparing sGC in each direction (i.e., forward-direction vs. backward-direction). To directly

566

compare sGC between conditions with unequal numbers of trials (i.e., correct vs. incorrect), we

567

subsampled trials according to the condition with a lower trial count. To ensure that results

568

reflected true directional effects, rather than between-area differences in signal-to-noise ratio,

569

we confirmed that estimated directionalities reversed when signals were time-reversed

570

(Supplementary Fig. 6). All analyses were conducted using the FieldTrip toolbox in MATLAB.

573

Partial correlations were computed to examine whether the strength of phase-amplitude

574

coupling was correlated during single trials. We used partial correlations, rather than standard

575

Pearson’s correlations, to control for potential sources of shared variance between variables

576

(i.e., theta, beta, and gamma amplitude). For each trial epoch and area, we computed single-

577

trial estimates for theta-beta MI, beta-gamma MI, theta amplitude, beta amplitude, and gamma

578

amplitude. Each variable was normalized by computing z-scores across all trials. Partial

579

580

theta, beta, and gamma amplitude. Correlation coefficients were averaged across sessions

581

using Fisher's z-transformation. All correlations were computed using the partialcorr function in

582

MATLAB.
