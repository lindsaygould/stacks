Weiyi Xie, MPH; Julie Nordgaard, MD, PhD, DMsc; R. Christopher Sheldrick, PhD; Juwairiya F. Ahmad, MPH; Fabiano A. Gomes, MD, PhD; Benicio N. Frey, MD, PhD; Laura Duncan, PhD

Abstract

IMPORTANCE Standardized diagnostic interviews (SDIs) are structured assessments based on established criteria to improve the consistency and reliability of diagnoses. The pooled test-retest reliability of SDIs for adult psychiatric disorders is unknown.

OBJECTIVES To estimate the test-retest reliability of SDIs used to classify common adult psychiatric disorders, examine variations in test-retest reliability between disorders, and assess prespecified factors associated with between-study heterogeneity.

DATA SOURCES MEDLINE, Embase, Emcare, PsycINFO, and Applied Social Sciences Index and Abstracts were searched without date or language limitations from inception until September 2025. References of eligible articles and relevant reviews were also screened.

STUDY SELECTION Primary studies that evaluated test-retest reliability of SDIs assessing adult psychiatric disorders were selected. Disorders were selected based on estimated prevalence in the general adult population, clinical relevance, and frequent appearance in SDIs.

DATA EXTRACTION AND SYNTHESIS Data were extracted and study quality was assessed based on the Consensus-based Standards for the Selection of Health Measurement Instruments checklist. Multilevel random-effects meta-analysis and meta-regression were performed. Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) guidelines were followed.

MAIN OUTCOMES AND MEASURES Test-retest reliability estimates (Cohen κ) of SDI-based adult psychiatric disorder diagnoses. Pooled estimates were calculated for 5 groups of mental disorders (anxiety, bipolar, depressive, personality, and nonaffective psychoses) and 8 groups of substance use disorders (SUDs; alcohol, cannabis, cocaine, hallucinogens, opioids, sedatives, stimulants, and tobacco).

RESULTS Fifty-seven studies were analyzed, 46 of which were included in the meta-analysis (535 κ estimates; N = 8146 participants \[mean age range, 22.0-54.3 years\]). The pooled estimate of SDI test-retest reliability was κ = 0.69 (95% CI, 0.66-0.72), with substantial between-study heterogeneity (Q534 = 23 578.7; P \< .001; I2 = 93%). Reliability was higher for SUDs than for mental disorders (κ = 0.72 \[95% CI, 0.69-0.72; 292 estimates\] vs 0.65 \[95% CI, 0.61-0.69; 243 estimates\]; z = 3.74; P \< .001) and varied among disorder types. Reliability for mental disorders ranged from κ = 0.55 (95% CI, 0.44-0.66) for nonaffective psychoses to κ = 0.74 (95% CI, 0.56-0.91) for bipolar disorders. Key Points

Question What is the pooled test-retest reliability of standardized diagnostic

interviews for common adult psychiatric disorders, including groups of mental disorders and substance use disorders (SUDs), and which factors explain between-study variation?

Findings In this systematic review and meta-analysis of 57 studies, the overall test-retest reliability of standardized diagnostic interviews was moderate and highly heterogeneous, with significantly higher reliability for SUDs than for mental disorders. Meaning The finding that standardized diagnostic interviews, although regarded as the gold standard of psychiatric disorder classification, had moderate and highly variable test-retest reliability highlights the need for careful selection and use.

Introduction

Psychiatric disorders, including both mental disorders and substance use disorders (SUDs), are associated with considerable global disease burden1 and increased risk of disability and premature mortality.2,3 Reliable and valid assessment of psychiatric disorders is critical to understand their epidemiologic characteristics and evaluate the service response. Built on established frameworks, existing measurements both reflect and affect our understanding of psychiatric disorders through their use in clinical settings (for diagnosis, treatment, and evaluation) and in the general population (for research, monitoring, resource allocation planning, and policy).4-7

Standardized diagnostic interviews (SDIs) were developed to standardize assessments leading to clinical diagnosis using criteria such as the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM)8 and the International Classification of Diseases (ICD)9,10 (eFigure 1 in Supplement 1 shows the timeline ofDSM and ICDcriteria evolution). SDIs were designed to improve the reliability of clinician diagnoses by reducing information variance, clinician bias, and inconsistent application of diagnostic criteria.11-14 SDIs are often described as a gold standard of psychiatric disorder classification,15despite concerns about the reliability and validity of classifications, their theoretical and practical adequacy, and evidence of limited reliability.12,16,17

Reliability, a prerequisite of validity, is the ability of an instrument to consistently differentiate between individuals based on their true differences.18 Standard metrics recognize that reliability can vary between raters (interrater reliability) and over time (test-retest reliability). For SDIs, reliability also depends on the administration method (independent retest interview vs original interview recoding).19Test-retest reliability, commonly estimated using Cohen κ,20provides a realistic estimate of reliability, accounting for random error associated with the interview being conducted at separate occasions by different interviewers. Cohen κ is typically interpreted as moderate or fair to moderate agreement at κ = 0.41 to 0.60 and as substantial or excellent agreement at κ = 0.61 to 0.80.21-24

Reliability is the proportion of the total variability (from respondent variability and measurement error) that is due to real variability between respondents. As measurement error increases, reliability decreases. Potential sources of random error or variability in SDIs include the interviewer, the interviewee, instrument characteristics, and interview context.25-27 There are statistical sources of variability, such as the way κ is calculated, and there are methodological sources of variability from study quality28-30(eAppendix 1 in Supplement 1 provides details). In clinical settings, SDI unreliability can cause disorder misclassification or misdiagnosis, leading to overtreatment or undertreatment. In research, SDI measurement error can bias effect size estimates, reduce the precision of findings, and mask true associations between variables.31-33

There is a need to understand the test-retest reliability of SDIs using systematic review and meta-analytic techniques to identify and pool evidence across SDIs. A similar investigation of SDIs assessing child and adolescent psychiatric disorders found that pooled reliability was moderate (mean κ = 0.58) and highly variable,34 but whether similar patterns exist in adult psychiatric disorder

Method

Protocol and Registration Study methods adhered to Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions.35 We followed the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) reporting guideline.36 This study was registered with the PROSPERO International Prospective Register of Systematic Reviews (Identifier: CRD42024517970).

Literature Search A systematic literature search with no date or language limitations was conducted in February 2024 and updated in September 2025 in MEDLINE, Embase, Emcare, PsycINFO, and Applied Social Sciences Index and Abstracts databases. We (W.X. and L.D.) developed the search strategy with consultation from 2 medical research librarians and using consistent and comprehensive terms applied across all disorders (eAppendix 2 in Supplement 1). Disorders were selected based on estimated prevalence in the general adult population,37 clinical relevance, and frequent appearance in SDIs based on preliminary searches. The selected disorders included depressive, anxiety, substance use, attention-deficit/hyperactivity, bipolar, schizophrenia, eating, personality, and conduct disorders. References of eligible articles and relevant reviews were also screened. Dissertations, books, comments, editorials, and letters were excluded.

Study Selection One of us (W.X.) screened titles and abstracts, and any uncertainties were discussed with a senior author (L.D.). Two of us (W.X. and J.F.A.) independently reviewed and assessed the eligibility of full texts based on the criteria outlined in eAppendix 3 in Supplement 1. Discrepancies were resolved through discussion. For included studies with missing data for meta-analysis, study authors were contacted using a protocol we established prior to our review.

Data Extraction Two of us (W.X. and J.F.A.) independently extracted data using a systematic review management system (Covidence; Veritas Health Innovation Ltd) as well as a data extraction tool and codebook (Microsoft Excel; Microsoft Corp). For each study sample and interview, only 1 κ estimate of test- retest reliability for each disorder per time reference period (eg, lifetime, past-month) was included. Disagreements were resolved through discussion. Interrater agreement was calculated for all key variables using an intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) for continuous variables and Cohen κ for categorical variables. Study characteristics, SDI characteristics, sample characteristics and methods, outcome measures, and study methodological quality indicators were extracted and coded prior to analysis (eAppendix 4 in Supplement 1).

Quality Assessment We extracted study characteristics associated with methodological quality and assessed their association with reliability using meta-regression. These quality indicators were adapted from the Consensus-based Standards for the Selection of Health Measurement Instruments (COSMIN) checklist for reliability studies, including appropriate sample size, appropriate and stated retest interval, independent interview administration, and reported response rate.38

Outlier and Influence Diagnostics To identify outliers or influential cases that might distort the results, outlier and influence diagnostics were conducted prior to analysis by examining Cook distances; leverage values; leave-one-out estimates; and DFBETAS, which measure changes in the pooled estimate after removing each study from the model.39 Profile likelihood plots were inspected for all multilevel random effects meta- analyses to ensure the plots peaked at the parameter estimates showing that the variance components of the models were identifiable.40

Statistical Analysis To account for within-study clustering and between-study heterogeneity, we used a 2-level multilevel random-effects model to pool κ estimates, wherein individual κ estimates were nested within study samples. Between-study variation was assessed using theQtest for heterogeneity and theI2statistic indicating the proportion of observed variance due to real differences in the estimates of κ, with anI2of 75% to 100% interpreted as considerable between-study heterogeneity.35To avoid double counting of κ estimates, estimates for any disorder or combined disorders were excluded from the analysis.

Expecting estimates of reliability to differ between disorders, subgroup analyses were conducted using random effects meta-analyses to estimate pooled κ estimates for groups of related mental disorders (attention-deficit/hyperactivity, anxiety, bipolar, conduct, depressive, eating, personality disorders, and nonaffective psychoses) and different SUD types (alcohol, cannabis, cocaine, hallucinogens, opioids, sedatives, stimulants, and tobacco), grouped as defined in theDSM and ICD (eTable 1 in Supplement 1). Tests of individual coefficients in the model (and corresponding CIs) were based on at-distribution for more conservative inference.39

To explore sources of between-study heterogeneity, we conducted univariate multilevel meta- regression analyses examining the impact of sample-level characteristics. Multivariate meta-regression was not performed due to sample size constraints at the study level. Univariate models were used to identify moderators with explanatory power atP \< .05,P \< .01, andP \< .001. ReportedPvalues were adjusted using the Benjamini-Hochberg method to account for multiple testing.41Because the pseudo- Knapp and Hartung modification to account for uncertainty in the heterogeneity estimate was unavail- able for multilevel models, Wald tests of individual coefficients were calculated and should be inter- preted cautiously, being less conservative than the adjusted approach.39

All analyses were conducted using the rma function and restricted maximum-likelihood estimation in the R package metafor (R Project for Statistical Computing), following the procedures for meta-analyzing Cohen κ recommended by Sun.42 Separate funnel plots for mental disorders and SUDs were created and visually inspected to assess potential publication bias and category-level heterogeneity. The regression test for funnel plot asymmetry is unavailable for multilevel random effects model and is designed primarily for use in reviews of randomized clinical trials.43

Identification of Moderators Candidate moderator variables were identified a priori from the literature (eAppendix 1 in Supplement 1), selected based on their availability following preliminary data extraction, and evaluated using the omnibus test for moderators (Qm).39 Moderators were categorized as (1) κ level, which was specific to individual κ estimates (eg, disorder group); (2) sample level, which was applicable to all κ estimates from a study (eg, SDI and diagnostic criteria); and (3) methodological quality indicators (eg, sample size and retest interval). Variable coding details are available in eAppendix 5 in Supplement 1. Following assertions that missing data are likely inconsequential at a rate of less than 5%,44 potential moderators with greater than 5% missing data were excluded.

Results

Study Selection The literature search identified 10 902 records, of which 10 855 (99.6%) were from 5 databases and 47 (0.4%) were from other sources (Figure 1). After title and abstract screening, 191 full-text articles were assessed and 57 met our inclusion criteria.45-101 Eleven45-55 of the 57 studies were excluded from the meta-analysis due to unavailable SEs of κ estimates, despite requests sent to study authors for this information. Four non-English studies (3 Chinese, 1 German)102-105that were eligible for full- text assessment were not screened due to a lack of resources to conduct translations, reviews, and duplicate extractions. Figure 1 shows the PRISMA flow diagram of the studies, and eAppendix 6 in Supplement 1 lists the 57 included articles.45-101

Interreviewer agreement was 100% for study inclusion and high for data extraction (κ = 0.83 \[95% CI, 0.46-1.00\] for categorical variables; ICC = 0.95 \[95% CI, 0.93-0.98\] for continuous variables; ICC = 0.91 \[95% CI, 0.84-0.95\] for extracted κ). We reached a consensus on all data extraction disagreements. The final meta-analysis sample included 535 κ estimates from 50 study samples in 46 articles.56-101 The total population included 8146 participants, with mean age ranging from 22.0 to 54.3 years; the proportion of males ranged from 0% to 97% among studies that reported participant characteristics. There were 292 κ estimates for SUDs and 243 κ estimates for mental disorders. Based on influential case diagnostics, 4 outlying cases were identified but not removed because they were valid data points and did not affect the overall estimates when omitted from the analysis (a difference in reliability estimate of 0.003 overall, 0.008 for mental disorders, and 0.008 for SUDs).

Synthesis of Results Pooled Estimates of SDI Test-Retest Reliability The overall test-retest reliability of SDIs across all disorders was estimated at κ = 0.69 (95% CI, 0.66- 0.72) based on 535 κ estimates.56-101Significant heterogeneity (Q534 = 23 578.7; P \< .001;I2 = 93%) indicated substantial between-study heterogeneity in κ estimates (Table 1). Meta-regression confirmed that disorder type significantly moderated reliability (Qm8 = 26.062; P = .001), reducing heterogeneity by 8.1%. As a result, random-effects multilevel meta-analyses and moderator analyses were conducted separately for mental disorders and SUDs. Test-retest reliability was significantly higher for SUDs than for mental disorders (κ = 0.72 \[95% CI, 0.69-0.76; 292 estimates\] vs κ = 0.65 \[95% CI, 0.61-0.69; 243 estimates\]; z = 3.74; P \< .001). Heterogeneity remained substantial for both mental disorders (Q242 = 6774.0; P \< .001; I2 = 94%) and SUDs (Q291 = 7722.2; P \< .001; I2 = 92%).

Subgroup Analyses Pooled estimates for 5 groups of mental disorders (anxiety, bipolar, depressive, personality, and nonaffective psychoses) and 8 groups of SUDs (alcohol, cannabis, cocaine, hallucinogens, opioids, sedatives, stimulants, and tobacco) are presented in Table 1 and Figure 2. Pooled estimates were not calculated for disorder groups with fewer than 10 κ estimates: conduct, attention-deficit/ hyperactivity, and eating disorders. Among mental disorders, pooled estimates ranged from κ = 0.55 (95% CI, 0.44-0.66) for nonaffective psychoses to κ = 0.74 (95% CI, 0.56-0.91) for bipolar disorder. In the SUD group, pooled estimates ranged from κ = 0.59 (95% CI, 0.49-0.70) for hallucinogens to κ = 0.81 (95% CI, 0.74-0.88) for opioids. Significant heterogeneity was found within all mental disorder and SUD subgroups, with heterogeneity ranging from 87% (personality disorders) to 98% (nonaffective psychoses) and 70% (hallucinogens) to 95% (opioids), respectively. Funnel plots for all disorders, mental disorders, and SUDs show some visual asymmetry (eFigure 2 in Supplement 1).

Moderator Analyses Results of univariate meta-regressions examining moderators of SDI test-retest reliability are presented inTable 2(for mental disorders) andTable 3(for SUDs). For mental disorders, no sample-level modera- tor or methodological quality indicator moderator was statistically significant. Substantial between- study heterogeneity remained after accounting for variability relating to the moderator. For SUDs, only diagnostic criteria were a statistically significant moderator, accounting for a 16% reduction in between- study variance. Compared with the reference category ofDSM-III, use ofDSM-III-R(Third Edition Re- vised) (β coefficient \[SE\] = 0.190 \[0.068\]; adjustedP = .02),DSM-IV(β coefficient \[SE\] = 0.159 \[0.068\]; adjustedP = .03), andICD-10(β coefficient \[SE\] = 0.172 \[0.069\]; adjustedP = .03) was associated with higher reliability. Other moderators were not significant, with substantial residual between-study hetero- geneity suggesting that additional unmeasured factors contribute to variation in SDI reliability.

Higher pooled reliability for SUDs than for mental disorders indicates that SDIs yield more stable results for psychiatric disorders with clearer behavioral or temporal criteria than psychiatric disorders requiring interpretive judgment of subjective experiences. Reliability also varied by disorder type, with nonaffective psychoses showing lower reliability than other mental disorders, indicating ongoing challenges in achieving consistent diagnostic judgments for complex and heterogeneous psychopathological conditions, even under structured assessment conditions. The observed funnel plot asymmetry may reflect small-study outcomes, heterogeneity across disorder categories (eFigure 2 in Supplement 1), or chance, rather than publication bias alone.106,107

Methodological quality indicators did not significantly moderate the reliability estimates, suggest- ing variability is not primarily attributable to study design. Instead, the substantial heterogeneity ob- served likely reflects the limitations inherent to current diagnostic systems and to the translation of clinical phenomena into standardized decision rules.108No moderators were identified for mental dis- orders; however, for SUDs, univariate meta-regression analysis revealed that diagnostic criteria used by SDIs explained some of the between-study heterogeneity in reliability. For SUDs only,ICD-10–based SDIs showed a slightly higher reliability compared withDSM-based SDIs.DSM-III–based SDIs had low reliability, and SDIs with more recent criteria such asDSM-5andDSM-IVshowed moderate to substan- tial reliability, butDSM-III-Runexpectedly achieved the highest reliability. The lower reliability forDSM- 5–based SDIs may reflect the challenges associated with theDSM-5SUD criteria, which combine the previously separate use and dependence diagnoses into a single category.109No included studies used the Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology criteria or Research Domain Criteria. These findings indicate the need to develop or revise SDIs based on newer diagnostic classification systems to improve their reliability.ICD-10–based interviews may be a better choice for SUD classification, althoughICD-10 is in the process of being replaced withICD, 11th Revision.

Other potential moderators—disorder prevalence, interview length, interview format (structured or semistructured), time reference period, and interviewer training—could not be examined due to insufficient data reported in the studies. Future psychometric studies of SDIs should follow reporting guidelines to improve the quality and completeness of meta-analytic evidence.

Implications The present study showed that, although SDIs are widely regarded as a methodological benchmark for ensuring diagnostic consistency, there was substantial variation in the test-retest reliability of SDIs. This finding suggests that SDIs do not confer a uniform level of reliability across psychiatric conditions and that their performance is affected by disorder type and underlying diagnostic complexity.

From a research perspective, the substantial heterogeneity observed indicates that the reliability estimates reported in initial validation studies may overstate the stability of SDI-based diagnoses when applied in diverse settings and populations. Caution is therefore warranted when using SDI-derived diagnoses as reference standards in etiological, neurobiological, or treatment studies. Clinically, the findings highlight that, while SDIs can facilitate systematic assessment, they cannot by themselves en- sure diagnostic precision, particularly for disorders characterized by phenomenological complexity or fluctuating course. Furthermore, if we consider the role of SDI reliability in sensitivity and specificity, assuming different levels of prevalence (eTable 5 in Supplement 1), readministration of a typical SDI (ie, getting a second clinical opinion) would result in reversing initial diagnostic findings for a substantial proportion of patients. These outcomes could be expected to be substantially higher if different SDIs were administered (thereby reducing method variance).

Limitations This study has several limitations. Incomplete reporting of data needed to calculate the SE of κ estimate led to the exclusion of 11 studies from the meta-analysis, although sensitivity analysis indicated that their exclusion had a minimal role in the pooled estimates. Conclusions

In this systematic review and meta-analysis of studies assessing common adult psychiatric disorders, SDIs showed moderate and heterogenous test-retest reliability that varied substantially across disorders. The findings underscore the need for a more nuanced understanding of diagnostic reliability in psychiatry, one that recognizes the limits of structural standardization and the potential value of integrating contextual and phenomenological information into diagnostic assessment and research practice.
