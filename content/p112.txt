A B S T R A C T

Background: Nasal Esketamine is a rapid-acting intervention for depression, hypothesized to exert its effects through cortical disinhibition. However, the spatio-temporal dynamics of brain network activity and their relationship to the subjective drug experience following Esketamine administration remain elusive. This obser- vational study aims to delineate brain network-level effects of Esketamine by analyzing changes in oscillatory and aperiodic EEG activity within 90 min following nasal Esketamine treatment in depression. Methods: Eight individuals with major depressive disorder undergoing regular Esketamine treatment participated in the study. High-density, eyes-open resting state EEG was recorded before and subsequently five times up to 90 min post-administration. Before each recording, individuals completed ratings of individual drug experience. EEG spectral features were separated into canonical frequency bands and aperiodic exponent. Primary outcomes were changes in neural activity and their association with individual drug experience. Results: Following Esketamine administration, we observed a marked decrease of frontoparietal alpha power and central beta and a significant increase of frontal midline delta and low gamma power, indicative of cortical disinhibition and reduction of top-down control. Correspondingly, the aperiodic exponent decreased, suggestive of a shift of the excitation/inhibition balance towards excitation. These electrophysiological changes were accompanied by an increase in subjective ratings of highness and happiness, and a decrease in tension. Further analyses revealed significant relationships between changes in neural activity and subjective drug experience. Conclusion: Our findings suggest that Esketamine induces brain network disinhibition and reduces top-down control. These neurophysiological changes closely correlate with individual drug experience, providing valu- able insights into the neural mechanisms underlying Esketamine’s immediate behavioral effects.

1\. Introduction

Since its initial FDA approval in 2019 (Havlik et al., 2023), Esket- amine, the S-Enantiomer of the dissociative anesthetic Ketamine, has been utilized as a new rapid-acting treatment for depression. Unlike traditional antidepressants that target monoamine neurotransmitters, Esketamine uniquely modulates glutamatergic signaling. In particular, its nasal application (Spravato) is increasingly used and has proven effective in reducing depression symptoms (Calder et al., 2024; Papa- kostas et al., 2020; Daly et al., 2019).

Nasal Esketamine has a rapid onset of action, reaching peak plasma

concentration within 20 to 40 min before declining (Perez-Ruixo et al., 2021; Walter et al., 2014). Its primary effects are thought to be due to preferential blocking of glutamate N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) re- ceptors on GABAergic interneurons (Zanos and Gould, 2018), leading to an increase in glutamatergic neurotransmission and network-level excitation via α-amino-3-hydroxy-5-methyl-4-isoxazolepropionic acid (AMPA) receptors (Lazzaro et al., 2003; Zhou et al., 2014). It has been hypothesized that the interaction of molecular effects leads to an overall shift in the excitation/inhibition (E/I) balance (Nguyen et al., 2023) causing network-level disinhibition, known as the disinhibition hy- pothesis (Zanos and Gould, 2018; Borsellino et al., 2023). 2.2. Experimental procedure

Researchers obtained a resting state EEG measurement (2 min, eyes- open) approximately 10 min before Esketamine administration (base- line). Five additional measurements (2 min, eyes-open) were taken at 15, 35, 50, 75, and 90 min post-Esketamine administration (Fig. 1).

2.3. Pre-treatment neuropsychological questionnaires

At the beginning of their in-person study session, participants completed the following computerized questionnaires: Patient Health Questionnaire (PHQ-9), Generalized Anxiety Disorder-7 (GAD-7), Snaith-Hamilton Pleasure Scale (SHAPS), State-Trait Anxiety Inventory (STAI), and Quality of Life, Enjoyment, and Satisfaction Questionnaire- Short Form (Q-LES-Q-SF). In accordance with their regular treatment protocol, individuals were assessed by their clinicians using the Ham- ilton Depression Rating Scale-17 (HDRS-17) prior to receiving Esketamine.

2.4. Individual drug experience and ketamine side effects

Participants rated their individual drug experiences on a scale from 0 to 100 using seven Visual Analogue Mood Scales (VAMS) (“happy”, “sad”, “calm”, “tense”, “energetic”, “sleepy”, plus “high”) before each EEG measurement (Luria, 1975). Additionally, researchers administered the Ketamine Side Effects Tool (KSET) at baseline, 50 and 90 min post- administration. In contrast to the KSET, which employs a 4-point scale and primarily focuses on physical symptoms, the VAMS provides a more nuanced assessment of current mood states through its continuous scale. Its simple design enables rapid administration, making it ideally suited for clinical settings and for repeated assessments of drug effects, including when participants are strongly affected by Esketamine (Arruda et al., 1996).

2.5. EEG recording and preprocessing

We collected eyes-open resting state EEG data using a high-density 128-channel system at 1000 Hz sampling rate (NetAmps 410, Elec- trical Geodesics Inc., OR, USA). Before the experiment, an electrolyte gel was administered below the electrodes to reduce impedances to \<50 kΩ. We implemented a semi-automatic preprocessing pipeline that included a high-pass filter at 1 Hz, a low-pass filter at 80 Hz, and com- mon average re-referencing. We automatically removed power line

artifacts and manually rejected noisy channels, which were then inter- polated spherically. After performing Independent Component Analysis (ICA), we used a combined automatic and manual component rejection process (see Supplementary Methods). Welch’s smooth spectral decomposition method was performed to estimate the power spectral density (PSD). Next, we used FOOOF toolbox (Donoghue et al., 2020) to fit the power spectra with aperiodic and periodic components with a fitting range of 2–80 Hz and a maximum of ten peaks allowed. We fitted all spectra with a fixed aperiodic mode, extracted the aperiodic signal, and analyzed the flattened PSD. From the aperiodic model fit, the aperiodic exponent was computed, which represents the slope of the linear model fit in log-log space. For analyses, the delta band was defined as 2–4 Hz, theta as 4–8 Hz, alpha as 8–12 Hz, beta as 12–30 Hz, low gamma as 30–58 Hz, and high gamma as 62–78 Hz.

2.6. Statistical analysis

Statistical analyses were conducted in MATLAB (version 2023) and R (version 4.1, R Core Team). The significance level was set at α \< 0.05 for all analyses.

2.6.1. EEG analysis

Flattened PSD and aperiodic exponent from 90 EEG head channels were extracted for each subject. Cluster permutation testing using the cluster\_perm1 function from the Mass Univariate ERP toolbox (version 1.0.4) across timepoints and channels was performed (Groppe et al., 2011) computing 10,000 permutations with a cluster significance level of 0.05, family-wise error rate of 0.05 and fixed seed. Clustermass correction was employed. Separately, we utilized a random-intercept Linear Mixed Effects Model (LMM) with treatment contrast including averaged whole-brain aperiodic exponent as the dependent variable and timepoint as a fixed effect predictor.

2.6.2. Behavioral analysis

We employed LMMs to investigate changes in individual drug experience over time, incorporating timepoint as fixed effect and random intercept for participant. Treatment contrasts were applied to compare subsequent timepoints with baseline. Due to the study’s exploratory nature, no correction for multiple comparisons was imple- mented in the behavioral analyses.

3.2. Esketamine effects on oscillatory activity

Separate cluster permutation tests identified significant clusters for a change in alpha, beta, low gamma and delta PSD up to 90 min post- Esketamine administration (Fig. 3). In contrast, theta and high gamma oscillations did not exhibit significant clusters. Specifically, alpha PSD showed a significant decrease that was maintained consistently at each measurement point up to 90 min (clustermass = − 1033.53, p = 0.014). Reduction started primarily occipital-parietal at 15 min, then extended to bilateral prefrontal cortices encompassing nearly all EEG head channels (35, 55 min). At 75 min and 90 min post-Esketamine admin- istration, the prefrontal cluster diminished, and the significant cluster predominantly included occipital-parietal channels again. Beta PSD significantly decreased post-Esketamine administration, exhibiting a significant cluster at each timepoint (clustermass = − 776.71, p = 0.007). The early cluster (15 min) included posterior and central- midline channels which extended into frontal midline channels. 4\. Discussion

following Esketamine administration in individuals with depression.

This observational study provides preliminary evidence of the potent effects of nasal Esketamine on oscillatory and aperiodic neural activity, persisting up to 90 min post-administration in individuals with depres- sion. The observed decrease in alpha power spectral density (PSD), in- crease in low gamma PSD, and decrease in the aperiodic exponent suggest an E/I balance shift towards excitation, supporting the disinhi- bition hypothesis. Importantly, our findings include insights into the spatial localization of these effects, which correlated with individual drug experience, establishing an immediate link between mesoscale neural activity and behavior on the macroscale. Specifically, the expe- rience of highness arose from the complex interplay of loss of long-range top-down control (alpha, beta), increase in local excitation (gamma, aperiodic exponent) and inhibition of sensory input (delta).

4.2.1. Gamma oscillations

We noted an increase in low gamma PSD directly following Esket- amine administration (15, 35 min). This increase was primarily located in the left frontal midline and gradually returned to baseline activity at 90 min. Given that gamma oscillations result from the coordinated interaction of local excitatory and inhibitory neural activity, which is modulated by inhibitory interneurons (Buzs´aki and Wang, 2012), the observed increase in gamma PSD could signify a local disinhibition of neural activity (Buzs´aki and Wang, 2012). Fittingly, a gamma power increase has also been linked to depression symptom improvement (Noda et al., 2017). Notably, the significant gamma cluster was located in the left-central prefrontal cortex (PFC), a region implicated in depression (Pizzagalli and Roberts, 2022) and identified as an effective treatment target for excitatory rTMS treatments (Schwippel et al., 2019). Apart from their sensorimotor func- tions, beta oscillations have been implicated in stabilizing the current brain state, top-down processing and long-range communication (Spitzer and Haegens, 2017). Interestingly, it has been proposed that an excess of beta oscillations could lead to an ultra-stable brain state, resulting in a decrease of adaptive cognition and behavior (Engel and Fries, 2010) – a state that closely mirrors a depression phenotype and stands in contrast to a detached state of highness.

4.2.3. Alpha oscillations

Following the administration of Esketamine, we observed an im- mediate and widespread decrease in alpha oscillations, which gradually faded. However, a significant cluster remained present in the occipital- parietal and left frontal areas at 90 min. Alpha oscillations, which gate information processing via inhibition (Jensen and Mazaheri, 2010; Laufs et al., 2003), also modulate local gamma activity and long-range gamma synchronization through cross-frequency coupling. As anticipated, we found an anticorrelation between low gamma and alpha PSD after Esketamine administration, suggesting a state of disinhibition and reduced top-down control in the cortex. In the context of depression, an abnormal increase of alpha oscillations, especially in the left PFC, has been documented (Fingelkurts et al., 2006), and the targeted reduction of alpha oscillations in the PFC using repeated non-invasive brain stimulation has been investigated as a new treatment approach (Alexander et al., 2019; Schwippel et al., 2024).

4.2.4. Delta oscillations

After a widespread increase at 15 min, delta PSD increase narrowed down to the left central PFC and reverted to baseline levels at 90 min. Interestingly, one could observe a temporal and spatial concurrence between the increase in delta and low gamma PSD. Also, the increase of theta PSD did not reach significance but exhibited a characteristic cen- tral midline pattern. Both oscillations have been implicated in guiding top-down behavior and integration of functional networks across brain areas (Helfrich et al., 2019), especially by hierarchically coordinating local gamma activity (Florin and Baillet, 2015). The correlation of delta PSD with highness could suggest that Esketamine shifts the brain’s state from engaged wakefulness towards a state of increasing detachment from external stimuli. Fittingly, studies report an increase in prefrontal delta oscillations during meditation (Faber et al., 2008), which was interpreted as a decoupling of brain networks leading to the subjective experience of detachment – a state not dissimilar to highness.

4.3. Aperiodic exponent

We observed an immediate reduction of the aperiodic exponent, most prominently in left central-frontal electrodes. This reduction diminished over time leaving a small significant cluster in the frontal- midline at 90 min. A decrease of the aperiodic exponent leads to a flatter power spectrum in the log-log scale, which has been linked to a E/ I balance shift towards excitation (Fig. 3) (Gao et al., 2017; Chini et al., 2022).
